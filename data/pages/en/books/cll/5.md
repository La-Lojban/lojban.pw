<span id="sec-5-1"></span><span id="section-content-words-brivla"></span>
### Lojban content words: brivla

At the center, logically and often physically, of every Lojban bridi is one or more words which constitute the selbri. A bridi expresses a relationship between things: the selbri specifies which relationship is referred to. The difference between:

<span id="ex-5-1"></span><span id="example-do-mamta-mi"></span>
> **do mamta mi**
> _You are-a-mother-of me_
> _You are my mother_

and

<span id="ex-5-2"></span><span id="example-random-id-B0aR"></span>
> **do patfu mi**
> _You are-a-father-of me._
> _You are my father._

lies in the different selbri.

The simplest kind of selbri is a single Lojban content word: a brivla. There are three different varieties of brivla: those which are built into the language (the gismu), those which are derived from combinations of the gismu (the lujvo), and those which are taken (usually in a modified form) from other languages (the fu'ivla). In addition, there are a few cmavo that can act like brivla; these are mentioned in [Sec. 5.9](/en/books/cll/5/#sec-5-9), and discussed in full in [Ch. 7](/en/books/cll/7/#ch-7).

For the purposes of this chapter, however, all brivla are alike. For example,

<span id="ex-5-3"></span><span id="example-random-id-483c"></span>
> **ta bloti**
> _That is-a-boat._
> _That is a boat._

<span id="ex-5-4"></span><span id="example-random-id-mdxB"></span>
> **ta brablo**
> _That is-a-large-boat._
> _That is a ship._

<span id="ex-5-5"></span><span id="example-random-id-UMjE"></span>
> **ta blotrskunri**
> _That is-a-(boat)-schooner._
> _That is a schooner._

illustrate the three types of brivla (gismu, lujvo, and fu'ivla respectively), but in each case the selbri is composed of a single word whose meaning can be learned independent of its origins.

The remainder of this chapter will mostly use gismu as example brivla, because they are short. However, it is important to keep in mind that wherever a gismu appears, it could be replaced by any other kind of brivla.

<span id="sec-5-2"></span><span id="section-simple-tanru"></span>
### Simple tanru

Beyond the single brivla, a selbri may consist of two brivla placed together. When a selbri is built in this way from more than one brivla, it is called a tanru, a word with no single English equivalent. The nearest analogue to tanru in English are combinations of two nouns such as _lemon tree_. There is no way to tell just by looking at the phrase _lemon tree_ exactly what it refers to, even if you know the meanings of _lemon_ and _tree_ by themselves. As English-speakers, we must simply know that it refers to _a tree which bears lemons as fruits_. A person who didn't know English very well might think of it as analogous to _brown tree_ and wonder, _What kind of tree is lemon-colored?_

In Lojban, tanru are also used for the same purposes as English adjective-noun combinations like _big boy_ and adverb-verb combinations like _quickly run_. This is a consequence of Lojban not having any such categories as _noun_, _verb_, _adjective_, or _adverb_. English words belonging to any of these categories are translated by simple brivla in Lojban. Here are some examples of tanru:

<span id="ex-5-6"></span><span id="example-lemon-tree"></span>
> **tu pelnimre tricu**
> _That-yonder is-a-lemon tree._
> _That is a lemon tree._

<span id="ex-5-7"></span><span id="example-random-id-qHNA"></span>
> **la djan. barda nanla**
> _That-named John is-a-big boy._
> _John is a big boy._

<span id="ex-5-8"></span><span id="example-random-id-eD63"></span>
> **mi sutra bajra**
> _I quick run_
> _I quickly run./I run quickly._

Note that **pelnimre** is a lujvo for _lemon_; it is derived from the gismu **pelxu**, yellow, and **nimre**, citrus. Note also that **sutra** can mean _fast/quick_ or _quickly_ depending on its use:

<span id="ex-5-9"></span><span id="example-random-id-cjhN"></span>
> **mi sutra**
> _I am-fast/quick_

shows **sutra** used to translate an adjective, whereas in [5.8](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-8) it is translating an adverb. (Another correct translation of [5.8](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-8), however, would be _I am a quick runner_.)

There are special Lojban terms for the two components of a tanru, derived from the place structure of the word **tanru**. The first component is called the **seltau**, and the second component is called the **tertau**.

The most important rule for use in interpreting tanru is that the tertau carries the primary meaning. A **pelnimre tricu** is primarily a tree, and only secondarily is it connected with lemons in some way. For this reason, an alternative translation of [5.6](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-6) would be:

<span id="ex-5-10"></span><span id="example-random-id-hP9j"></span>
> _That is a lemon type of tree._

This _type of_ relationship between the components of a tanru is fundamental to the tanru concept.

We may also say that the seltau modifies the meaning of the tertau:

<span id="ex-5-11"></span><span id="example-random-id-4fvn"></span>
> _That is a tree which is lemon-ish (in the way appropriate to trees)_

would be another possible translation of [5.6](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-6). In the same way, a more explicit translation of [5.7](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-7) might be:

<span id="ex-5-12"></span><span id="example-random-id-asRA"></span>
> _John is a boy who is big in the way that boys are big._

This _way that boys are big_ would be quite different from the way in which elephants are big; big-for-a-boy is small-for-an-elephant.

All tanru are ambiguous semantically. Possible translations of:

<span id="ex-5-13"></span><span id="example-random-id-aIfM"></span>
> **ta klama jubme**
> _That is-a-goer type-of-table._

include:

- That is a table which goes (a wheeled table, perhaps).
- That is a table owned by one who goes.
- That is a table used by those who go (a sports doctor's table?).
- That is a table when it goes (otherwise it is a chair?).

In each case the object referred to is a _goer type of table_, but the ambiguous _type of_ relationship can mean one of many things. A speaker who uses tanru (and pragmatically all speakers must) takes the risk of being misunderstood. Using tanru is convenient because they are short and expressive; the circumlocution required to squeeze out all ambiguity can require too much effort.

No general theory covering the meaning of all possible tanru exists; probably no such theory can exist. However, some regularities obviously do exist:

<span id="ex-5-14"></span><span id="example-random-id-Lczh"></span>
> **do barda prenu**
> _You are-a-large person._

<span id="ex-5-15"></span><span id="example-random-id-Ldb1"></span>
> **do cmalu prenu**
> _You are-a-small person._

are parallel tanru, in the sense that the relationship between **barda** and **prenu** is the same as that between **cmalu** and **prenu**. [Sec. 5.14](/en/books/cll/5/#sec-5-14) and [Sec. 5.15](/en/books/cll/5/#sec-5-15) contain a partial listing of some types of tanru, with examples.

### Three-part tanru grouping with bo

The following cmavo is discussed in this section:

<tbody>

<td>**bo**</td>
<td>`BO`</td>
<td>closest scope grouping</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

Consider the English sentence:

<span id="ex-5-16"></span><span id="example-random-id-gCLr"></span>
> _That's a little girls' school._

What does it mean? Two possible readings are:

<span id="ex-5-17"></span><span id="example-random-id-5UBW"></span>
> _That's a little school for girls._

<span id="ex-5-18"></span><span id="example-random-id-5UBJ"></span>
> _That's a school for little girls._

This ambiguity is quite different from the simple tanru ambiguity described in [Sec. 5.2](/en/books/cll/5/#sec-5-2). We understand that _girls' school_ means _a school where girls are the students_, and not _a school where girls are the teachers_ or _a school which is a girl_ (!). Likewise, we understand that _little girl_ means _girl who is small_. This is an ambiguity of grouping. Is _girls' school_ to be taken as a unit, with _little_ specifying the type of girls' school? Or is _little girl_ to be taken as a unit, specifying the type of school? In English speech, different tones of voice, or exaggerated speech rhythm showing the grouping, are used to make the distinction; English writing usually leaves it unrepresented.

Lojban makes no use of tones of voice for any purpose; explicit words are used to do the work. The cmavo **bo** (which belongs to selma'o BO) may be placed between the two brivla which are most closely associated. Therefore, a Lojban translation of [5.17](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-17) would be:

<span id="ex-5-19"></span><span id="example-random-id-nwuU"></span>
> **ta cmalu nixli bo ckule**
> _That is-a-small girl [-] school._

[5.18](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-18) might be translated:

<span id="ex-5-20"></span><span id="example-random-id-jquh"></span>
> **ta cmalu bo nixli ckule**
> _That is-a-small [-] girl school._

The **bo** is represented in the literal translation by a bracketed hyphen (not to be confused with the bare hyphen used as a placeholder in other glosses) because in written English a hyphen is sometimes used for the same purpose: _a big dog-catcher_ would be quite different from a _big-dog catcher_ (presumably someone who catches only big dogs).

Analysis of [5.19](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-19) and [5.20](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-20) reveals a tanru nested within a tanru. In [5.19](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-19), the main tanru has a seltau of **cmalu** and a tertau of **nixli bo ckule**; the tertau is itself a tanru with **nixli** as the seltau and **ckule** as the tertau. In [5.20](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-20), on the other hand, the seltau is **cmalu bo nixli** (itself a tanru), whereas the tertau is **ckule**. This structure of tanru nested within tanru forms the basis for all the more complex types of selbri that will be explained below.

What about [5.21](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-21)? What does it mean?

<span id="ex-5-21"></span><span id="example-random-id-9FPm"></span>
> **ta cmalu nixli ckule**
> _That is-a-small girl school._

The rules of Lojban do not leave this sentence ambiguous, as the rules of English do with [5.16](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-16). The choice made by the language designers is to say that [5.21](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-21) means the same as [5.20](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-20). This is true no matter what three brivla are used: the leftmost two are always grouped together. This rule is called the _left-grouping rule_. Left-grouping in seemingly ambiguous structures is quite common – though not universal – in other contexts in Lojban.

Another way to express the English meaning of [5.19](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-19) and [5.20](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-20), using parentheses to mark grouping, is:

<span id="ex-5-22"></span><span id="example-random-id-ERBx"></span>
> **ta cmalu - nixli bo ckule**
> _That is-a-small type-of (girl type-of school)._

<span id="ex-5-23"></span><span id="example-random-id-ERBQ"></span>
> **ta cmalu bo nixli - ckule**
> _That is-a-(small type-of girl) type-of school._

Because _type-of_ is implicit in the Lojban tanru form, it has no Lojban equivalent.

Note: It is perfectly legal, though pointless, to insert **bo** into a simple tanru:

<span id="ex-5-24"></span><span id="example-random-id-q6br"></span>
> **ta klama bo jubme**
> _That is-a-goer [-] table._

is a legal Lojban bridi that means exactly the same thing as [5.13](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-13), and is ambiguous in exactly the same ways. The cmavo **bo** serves only to resolve grouping ambiguity: it says nothing about the more basic ambiguity present in all tanru.

<span id="sec-5-4"></span><span id="section-complex-grouping"></span>
### Complex tanru grouping

If one element of a tanru can be another tanru, why not both elements?

<span id="ex-5-25"></span><span id="example-random-id-m5SD"></span>
> **do mutce bo barda gerku bo kavbu**
> _You are-a-(very type-of large) (dog type-of capturer)._
> _You are a very large dog-catcher._

In [5.25](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-25), the selbri is a tanru with seltau **mutce bo barda** and tertau **gerku bo kavbu**. It is worth emphasizing once again that this tanru has the same fundamental ambiguity as all other Lojban tanru: the sense in which the _dog type-of capturer_ is said to be _very type-of large_ is not precisely specified. Presumably it is his body which is large, but theoretically it could be one of his other properties.

We will now justify the title of this chapter by exploring the ramifications of the phrase _pretty little girls' school_, an expansion of the tanru used in [Sec. 5.3](/en/books/cll/5/#sec-5-3) to four brivla. (Although this example has been used in the Loglan Project almost since the beginning – it first appeared in Quine's book _Word and Object_ (1960) – it is actually a mediocre example because of the ambiguity of English _pretty_; it can mean _beautiful_, the sense intended here, or it can mean _very_. Lojban **melbi** is not subject to this ambiguity: it means only _beautiful_.)

Here are four ways to group this phrase:

<span id="ex-5-26"></span><span id="example-random-id-qjmr"></span>
> **ta melbi - cmalu - nixli - ckule**
> _That is-a-((pretty type-of little) type-of girl) type-of school._
> _That is a school for girls who are beautifully small._

<span id="ex-5-27"></span><span id="example-random-id-qjNi"></span>
> **ta melbi - cmalu nixli bo ckule**
> _That is-a-(pretty type-of little) (girl type-of school)._
> _That is a girls' school which is beautifully small._

<span id="ex-5-28"></span><span id="example-random-id-qjog"></span>
> **ta melbi - cmalu bo nixli - ckule**
> _That is-a-(pretty type-of (little type-of girl)) type-of school._
> _That is a school for small girls who are beautiful._

<span id="ex-5-29"></span><span id="example-random-id-qjop"></span>
> **ta melbi - cmalu bo nixli bo ckule**
> _That is-a-pretty type-of (little type-of (girl type-of school))._
> _That is a small school for girls which is beautiful._

[5.29](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-29) uses a construction which has not been seen before: **cmalu bo nixli bo ckule**, with two consecutive uses of **bo** between brivla. The rule for multiple **bo** constructions is the opposite of the rule when no **bo** is present at all: the last two are grouped together. Not surprisingly, this is called the _right-grouping rule_, and it is associated with every use of **bo** in the language. Therefore,

<span id="ex-5-30"></span><span id="example-random-id-snKn"></span>
> **ta cmalu bo nixli bo ckule**
> _That is-a-little type-of (girl type-of school)._

means the same as [5.19](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-19), not [5.20](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-20). This rule may seem peculiar at first, but one of its consequences is that **bo** is never necessary between the first two elements of any of the complex tanru presented so far: all of [5.26](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-26) through [5.29](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-29) could have **bo** inserted between **melbi** and **cmalu** with no change in meaning.

### Complex tanru with ke and ke'e

There is, in fact, a fifth grouping of _pretty little girls' school_ that cannot be expressed with the resources explained so far. To handle it, we must introduce the grouping parentheses cmavo, **ke** and **ke'e** (belonging to selma'o KE and KEhE respectively). Any portion of a selbri sandwiched between these two cmavo is taken to be a single tanru component, independently of what is adjacent to it. Thus, [5.26](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-26) can be rewritten in any of the following ways:

<span id="ex-5-31"></span><span id="example-random-id-qjqu"></span>
> **ta ke melbi cmalu ke'e nixli ckule**
> _That is-a-( pretty little ) girl school._

<span id="ex-5-32"></span><span id="example-random-id-qJQz"></span>
> **ta ke ke melbi cmalu ke'e nixli ke'e ckule**
> _That is-a-( ( pretty little ) girl ) school._

<span id="ex-5-33"></span><span id="example-random-id-qjSA"></span>
> **ta ke ke ke melbi cmalu ke'e nixli ke'e ckule ke'e**
> _That is-a-( ( ( pretty little ) girl ) school )._

Even more versions could be created simply by placing any number of **ke** cmavo at the beginning of the selbri, and a like number of **ke'e** cmavo at its end. Obviously, all of these are a waste of breath once the left-grouping rule has been grasped. However, the following is equivalent to [5.28](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-28) and may be easier to understand:

<span id="ex-5-34"></span><span id="example-random-id-zV26"></span>
> **ta - melbi - ke cmalu - nixli ke'e - - ckule**
> _That is-a-( pretty type-of ( little type-of girl ) ) type-of school._

Likewise, a **ke** and **ke'e** version of [5.27](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-27) would be:

<span id="ex-5-35"></span><span id="example-random-id-AUdM"></span>
> **ta melbi - cmalu ke nixli - ckule [ke'e]**
> _That is-a-(pretty type-of little) ( girl type-of school )._

The final **ke'e** is given in square brackets here to indicate that it can be elided. It is always possible to elide **ke'e** at the end of the selbri, making [5.35](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-35) as terse as [5.27](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-27).

Now how about that fifth grouping? It is

<span id="ex-5-36"></span><span id="example-random-id-tz0L"></span>
> **ta melbi - ke - cmalu - nixli - - ckule [ke'e]**
> _That is-a-pretty type-of ( ( little type-of girl ) type-of school )._
> _That is a beautiful school for small girls._

[5.36](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-36) is distinctly different in meaning from any of [5.26](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-26) through [5.29](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-29). Note that within the **ke**…**ke'e** parentheses, the left-grouping rule is applied to **cmalu nixli ckule**.

It is perfectly all right to mix **bo** and **ke**…**ke'e** in a single selbri. For instance, [5.29](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-29), which in pure **ke**…**ke'e** form is

<span id="ex-5-37"></span><span id="example-random-id-uBS4"></span>
> **ta melbi - ke cmalu**
> _That is-a-pretty type-of ( little_

can equivalently be expressed as:

<span id="ex-5-38"></span><span id="example-random-id-Ei5U"></span>
> **ta melbi - ke cmalu - nixli bo ckule [ke'e]**
> _That is-a-pretty type-of ( little type-of-( girl type-of school ))._

and in many other different forms as well.

Consider the English phrase _big red dog_. How shall this be rendered as a Lojban tanru? The naive attempt:

<span id="ex-5-39"></span><span id="example-random-id-riAq"></span>
> **barda - xunre - gerku**
> _(big type-of red) type-of dog_

will not do, as it means a dog whose redness is big, in whatever way redness might be described as _big_. Nor is

<span id="ex-5-40"></span><span id="example-random-id-6MqF"></span>
> **barda - xunre bo gerku**
> _big type-of (red type-of dog)_

much better. After all, the straightforward understanding of the English phrase is that the dog is big as compared with other dogs, not merely as compared with other red dogs. In fact, the bigness and redness are independent properties of the dog, and only obscure rules of English adjective ordering prevent us from saying _red big dog_.

The Lojban approach to this problem is to introduce the cmavo **je**, which is one of the many equivalents of English _and_. A big red dog is one that is both big and red, and we can say:

<span id="ex-5-41"></span><span id="example-random-id-0UrF"></span>
> **barda je xunre - gerku**
> _(big and red) type-of dog_

Of course,

<span id="ex-5-42"></span><span id="example-random-id-DzeP"></span>
> **xunre je barda - gerku**
> _(red and big) type-of dog_

is equally satisfactory and means the same thing. As these examples indicate, joining two brivla with **je** makes them a unit for tanru purposes. However, explicit grouping with **bo** or **ke**…**ke'e** associates brivla more closely than **je** does:

<span id="ex-5-43"></span><span id="example-random-id-LES9"></span>
> **barda je pelxu bo xunre gerku**
> _(big and (yellow type-of red)) dog_

With no grouping indicators, we get:

<span id="ex-5-44"></span><span id="example-random-id-fuhg"></span>
> **barda je pelxu - xunre - gerku**
> _((big and yellow) type-of red) type-of dog_
> _biggish- and yellowish-red dog_

which again raises the question of [5.39](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-39): what does _biggish-red_ mean?

Unlike **bo** and **ke**…**ke'e**, **je** is useful as well as merely legal within simple tanru. It may be used to partly resolve the ambiguity of simple tanru:

<span id="ex-5-45"></span><span id="example-random-id-W56H"></span>
> **ta blanu je zdani**
> _that is-blue and is-a-house_

definitely refers to something which is both blue and is a house, and not to any of the other possible interpretations of simple **blanu zdani**. Furthermore, **blanu zdani** refers to something which is blue in the way that houses are blue; **blanu je zdani** has no such implication – the blueness of a **blanu je zdani** is independent of its houseness.

With the addition of **je**, many more versions of _pretty little girls' school_ are made possible: see [Sec. 5.16](/en/books/cll/5/#sec-5-16) for a complete list.

A subtle point in the semantics of tanru like [5.41](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-41) needs special elucidation. There are at least two possible interpretations of:

<span id="ex-5-46"></span><span id="example-random-id-N5Bt"></span>
> **ta melbi je nixli - ckule**
> _That is-a-(beautiful and girl) type-of school._

It can be understood as:

<span id="ex-5-47"></span><span id="example-random-id-FCDa"></span>
> _That is a girls' school and a beautiful school._

or as:

<span id="ex-5-48"></span><span id="example-random-id-aFxm"></span>
> _That is a school for things which are both girls and beautiful._

The interpretation specified by [5.47](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-47) treats the tanru as a sort of abbreviation for:

<span id="ex-5-49"></span><span id="example-random-id-pHHw"></span>
> **ta ke melbi - ckule ke'e je ke nixli - ckule [ke'e]**
> _That is-a-( beautiful type-of school ) and ( girl type-of school )_

whereas the interpretation specified by [5.48](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-48) does not. This is a kind of semantic ambiguity for which Lojban does not compel a firm resolution. The way in which the school is said to be of type _beautiful and girl_ may entail that it is separately a beautiful school and a girls' school; but the alternative interpretation, that the members of the school are beautiful and girls, is also possible. Still another interpretation is:

<span id="ex-5-50"></span><span id="example-random-id-2cjH"></span>
> _That is a school for beautiful things and also for girls._

so while the logical connectives help to resolve the meaning of tanru, they by no means compel a single meaning in and of themselves.

In general, logical connectives within tanru cannot undergo the formal manipulations that are possible with the related logical connectives that exist outside tanru; see [Sec. 14.12](/en/books/cll/14/#sec-14-12) for further details.

The logical connective **je** is only one of the fourteen logical connectives that Lojban provides. Here are a few examples of some of the others:

<span id="ex-5-51"></span><span id="example-random-id-qJse"></span>
> **le bajra cu jinga ja te jinga**
> _the runner(s) is/are winner(s) or loser(s)._

<span id="ex-5-52"></span><span id="example-random-id-qJsg"></span>
> **blanu naja lenku skapi**
> _(blue only-if cold) skin_
> _skin which is blue only if it is cold_

<span id="ex-5-53"></span><span id="example-random-id-qjsy"></span>
> **xamgu jo tordu nuntavla**
> _(good if-and-only-if short) speech_
> _speech which is good if (and only if) it is short_

<span id="ex-5-54"></span><span id="example-random-id-qjtD"></span>
> **vajni ju pluka nuntavla**
> _(important whether-or-not pleasing) event-of-talking_
> _speech which is important, whether or not it is pleasing_

In [5.51](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-51), **ja** is grammatically equivalent to **je** but means _or_ (more precisely, _and/or_). Likewise, **naja** means _only if_ in [5.52](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-52), **jo** means _if and only if_ in [5.53](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-53), and **ju** means _whether or not_ in [5.54](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-54).

Now consider the following example:

<span id="ex-5-55"></span><span id="example-random-id-NuWM"></span>
> **ricfu je blanu jabo crino**
> _rich and (blue or green)_

which illustrates a new grammatical feature: the use of both **ja** and **bo** between tanru components. The two cmavo combine to form a compound whose meaning is that of **ja** but which groups more closely; **ja****bo** is to **ja** as plain **bo** is to no cmavo at all. However, both **ja** and **ja****bo** group less closely than **bo** does:

<span id="ex-5-56"></span><span id="example-random-id-KxqX"></span>
> **ricfu je blanu jabo crino bo blanu**
> _rich and (blue or green [-] blue)_
> _rich and (blue or greenish-blue)_

An alternative form of [5.55](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-55) is:

<span id="ex-5-57"></span><span id="example-random-id-2WtT"></span>
> **ricfu je ke blanu ja crino [ke'e]**
> _rich and ( blue or green )_

In addition to the logical connectives, there are also a variety of non-logical connectives, grammatically equivalent to the logical ones. The only one with a well-understood meaning in tanru contexts is **joi**, which is the kind of _and_ that denotes a mixture:

<span id="ex-5-58"></span><span id="example-random-id-Hr1L"></span>
> **ti blanu joi xunre bolci**
> _This is-a-(blue and red) ball._

The ball described is neither solely red nor solely blue, but probably striped or in some other way exhibiting a combination of the two colors. [5.58](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-58) is distinct from:

<span id="ex-5-59"></span><span id="example-random-id-NAhT"></span>
> **ti blanu xunre bolci**
> _This is a bluish-red ball_

which would be a ball whose color is some sort of purple tending toward red, since **xunre** is the more important of the two components. On the other hand,

<span id="ex-5-60"></span><span id="example-random-id-78C3"></span>
> **ti blanu je xunre bolci**
> _This is-a-(blue and red) ball_

is probably self-contradictory, seeming to claim that the ball is independently both blue and red at the same time, although some sensible interpretation may exist.

Finally, just as English _and_ has the variant form _both ... and_, so **je** between tanru components has the variant form **gu'e**…**gi**, where **gu'e** is placed before the components and **gi** between them:

<span id="ex-5-61"></span><span id="example-random-id-gLbh"></span>
> **gu'e barda gi xunre - gerku**
> _(both big and red) type-of dog_

is equivalent in meaning to [5.41](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-41). For each logical connective related to **je**, there is a corresponding connective related to **gu'e**…**gi** in a systematic way.

The portion of a **gu'e**…**gi** construction before the **gi** is a full selbri, and may use any of the selbri resources including **je** logical connections. After the **gi**, logical connections are taken to be wider in scope than the **gu'e**…**gi**, which has in effect the same scope as **bo**:

<span id="ex-5-62"></span><span id="example-random-id-ETVe"></span>
> **gu'e barda je xunre gi gerku ja mlatu**
> _(both (big and red) and dog) or cat_
> _something which is either big, red, and a dog, or else a cat_

leaves **mlatu** outside the **gu'e**…**gi** construction. The scope of the **gi** arm extends only to a single brivla or to two or more brivla connected with **bo** or **ke**…**ke'e**.

<span id="sec-5-7"></span><span id="section-be-sumti"></span>
### Linked sumti: be-bei-be'o

The following cmavo are discussed in this section:

<table>
<tbody>
<tr>
<td>**be**</td>
<td>`BE`</td>
<td>linked sumti marker</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>**bei**</td>
<td>`BEI`</td>
<td>linked sumti separator</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>**be'o**</td>
<td>`BEhO`</td>
<td>linked sumti terminator</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

The question of the place structures of selbri has been glossed over so far. This chapter does not attempt to treat place structure issues in detail; they are discussed in [Ch. 9](/en/books/cll/9/#ch-9). One grammatical structure related to places belongs here, however. In simple sentences such as [5.1](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-1), the place structure of the selbri is simply the defined place structure of the gismu **mamta**. What about more complex selbri?

For tanru, the place structure rule is simple: the place structure of a tanru is always the place structure of its tertau. Thus, the place structure of **blanu zdani** is that of **zdani**: the x1 place is a house or nest, and the x2 place is its occupants.

What about the places of **blanu**? Is there any way to get them into the act? In fact, **blanu** has only one place, and this is merged, as it were, with the x1 place of **zdani**. It is whatever is in the x1 place that is being characterized as blue-for-a-house. But if we replace **blanu** with **xamgu**, we get:

<span id="ex-5-63"></span><span id="example-random-id-tffW"></span>
> **ti xamgu zdani**
> _This is-a-good house._
> _This is a good (for someone, by some standard) house._

Since **xamgu** has three places (x1, the good thing; x2, the person for whom it is good; and x3, the standard of goodness), [5.63](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-63) necessarily omits information about the last two: there is no room for them. Room can be made, however!

<span id="ex-5-64"></span><span id="example-random-id-Uuio"></span>
> **ti xamgu be do bei mi [be'o] zdani**
> _This is-a-good (for you by-standard me) - house._
> _This is a house that is good for you by my standards._

Here, the gismu **xamgu** has been followed by the cmavo **be** (of selma'o BE), which signals that one or more sumti follows. These sumti are not part of the overall bridi place structure, but fill the places of the brivla they are attached to, starting with x2. If there is more than one sumti, they are separated by the cmavo **bei** (of selma'o BEI), and the list of sumti is terminated by the elidable terminator **be'o** (of selma'o BEhO).

Grammatically, a brivla with sumti linked to it in this fashion plays the same role in tanru as a simple brivla. To illustrate, here is a fully fleshed-out version of [5.19](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-19), with all places filled in:

<span id="ex-5-65"></span><span id="example-random-id-7vxB"></span>
> **ti cmalu be le ka canlu**
> _This is-a-small (in-dimension the property-of volume_

Here the three places of **cmalu**, the three of **nixli**, and the four of **ckule** are fully specified. Since the places of **ckule** are the places of the bridi as a whole, it was not necessary to link the sumti which follow **ckule**. It would have been legal to do so, however:

<span id="ex-5-66"></span><span id="example-random-id-YIty"></span>
> **mi klama be le zarci bei le zdani [be'o]**
> _I go ( to-the market - from-the house )._

means the same as

<span id="ex-5-67"></span><span id="example-random-id-UtBR"></span>
> **mi klama le zarci le zdani**
> _I go to-the market from-the house._

No matter how complex a tanru gets, the last brivla always dictates the place structure: the place structure of

<span id="ex-5-68"></span><span id="example-random-id-Twmx"></span>
> **- melbi je cmalu nixli bo ckule**
> _a (pretty and little) (girl - school)_
> _a school for girls which is both beautiful and small_

is simply that of **ckule**. (The sole exception to this rule is discussed in [Sec. 5.8](/en/books/cll/5/#sec-5-8).)

It is possible to precede linked sumti by the place structure ordering tags **fe**, **fi**, **fo**, and **fu** (of selma'o FA, discussed further in [Sec. 9.3](/en/books/cll/9/#sec-9-3)), which serve to explicitly specify the x2, x3, x4, and x5 places respectively. Normally, the place following the **be** is the x2 place and the other places follow in order. If it seems convenient to change the order, however, it can be accomplished as follows:

<span id="ex-5-69"></span><span id="example-random-id-mhS7"></span>
> **ti xamgu be fi mi bei fe do [be'o] zdani**
> _This is-a-good ( by-standard me - for you ) house._

which is equivalent in meaning to [5.64](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-64). Note that the order of **be**, **bei**, and **be'o** does not change; only the inserted **fi** tells us that **mi** is the x3 place (and correspondingly, the inserted **fe** tells us that **do** is the x2 place). Changing the order of sumti is often done to match the order of another language, or for emphasis or rhythm.

Of course, using FA cmavo makes it easy to specify one place while omitting a previous place:

<span id="ex-5-70"></span><span id="example-random-id-9b37"></span>
> **ti xamgu be fi mi [be'o] zdani**
> _This is-a-good ( by-standard me ) house._
> _This is a good house by my standards._

Similarly, sumti labeled by modal or tense tags can be inserted into strings of linked sumti just as they can into bridi:

<span id="ex-5-71"></span><span id="example-random-id-GstI"></span>
> **ta blanu be ga'a mi [be'o] zdani**
> _That is-a-blue ( to-observer me ) house._
> _That is a blue, as I see it, house._

The meaning of [5.71](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-71) is slightly different from:

<span id="ex-5-72"></span><span id="example-random-id-63c5"></span>
> **ta blanu zdani ga'a mi**
> _That is-a-blue house to-observer me._
> _That is a blue house, as I see it._

See discussions in [Ch. 9](/en/books/cll/9/#ch-9) of modals and in [Ch. 10](/en/books/cll/10/#ch-10) of tenses for more explanations.

The terminator **be'o** is almost always elidable: however, if the selbri belongs to a description, then a relative clause following it will attach to the last linked sumti unless **be'o** is used, in which case it will attach to the outer description:

<span id="ex-5-73"></span><span id="example-random-id-qJTI"></span>
> **le xamgu be do noi barda cu zdani**
> _The good-thing for you (who are-large) - is-a-house._

<span id="ex-5-74"></span><span id="example-random-id-qjTj"></span>
> **le xamgu be do be'o noi barda cu zdani**
> _The (good-thing for you ) (which is-large) - is-a-house_

(Relative clauses are explained in [Ch. 8](/en/books/cll/8/#ch-8).)

In other cases, however, **be'o** cannot be elided if **ku** has also been elided:

<span id="ex-5-75"></span><span id="example-random-id-zb4A"></span>
> **le xamgu be le ctuca [ku] be'o zdani**
> _the good (for the teacher) - - house_

requires either **ku** or **be'o**, and since there is only one occurrence of **be**, the **be'o** must match it, whereas it may be confusing which occurrence of **le** the **ku** terminates (in fact the second one is correct).

<span id="sec-5-8"></span><span id="section-co-inversion"></span>
### Inversion of tanru: co

The following cmavo is discussed in this section:

<table>
<tbody>
<tr>
<td>**co**</td>
<td>`CO`</td>
<td>tanru inversion marker</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

The standard order of Lojban tanru, whereby the modifier precedes what it modifies, is very natural to English-speakers: we talk of _blue houses_, not of _houses blue_. In other languages, however, such matters are differently arranged, and Lojban supports this reverse order (tertau before seltau) by inserting the particle **co**. [5.76](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-76) and [5.77](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-77) mean exactly the same thing:

<span id="ex-5-76"></span><span id="example-random-id-qjtV"></span>
> **ta blanu zdani**
> _That is-a-blue type-of-house._
> _That is a blue house._

<span id="ex-5-77"></span><span id="example-random-id-qjty"></span>
> **ta zdani co blanu**
> _That is-a-house of-type blue._
> _That is a blue house._

This change is called _tanru inversion_. In tanru inversion, the element before **co** (**zdani** in [5.77](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-77)) is the tertau, and the element following **co** (**blanu**) in [5.77](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-77)) is the seltau.

The meaning, and more specifically, the place structure, of a tanru is not affected by inversion: the place structure of **zdani co blanu** is still that of **zdani**. However, the existence of inversion in a selbri has a very special effect on any sumti which follow that selbri. Instead of being interpreted as filling places of the selbri, they actually fill the places (starting with x2) of the seltau. In [Sec. 5.7](/en/books/cll/5/#sec-5-7), we saw how to fill interior places with **be**…**bei**…**be'o**, and in fact [5.78](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-78) and [5.79](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-79) have the same meaning:

<span id="ex-5-78"></span><span id="example-random-id-qjuc"></span>
> **mi klama be le zarci bei le zdani be'o troci**
> _I am-a-(goer to the market from the house ) type-of-trier._
> _I try to go to the market from the house._

<span id="ex-5-79"></span><span id="example-random-id-qjVx"></span>
> **mi troci co klama le zarci le zdani**
> _I am-a-trier of-type (goer to-the market from-the house)._
> _I try to go to the market from the house._

[5.79](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-79) is a less deeply nested construction, requiring fewer cmavo. As a result it is probably easier to understand.

Note that in Lojban _trying to go_ is expressed using **troci** as the tertau. The reason is that _trying to go_ is a _going type of trying_, not a _trying type of going_. The trying is more fundamental than the going – if the trying fails, we may not have a going at all.

Any sumti which precede a selbri with an inverted tanru fill the places of the selbri (i.e., the places of the tertau) in the ordinary way. In [5.79](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-79), **mi** fills the x1 place of **troci co klama**, which is the x1 place of **troci**. The other places of the selbri remain unfilled. The trailing sumti **le zarci** and **le zdani** do not occupy selbri places, despite appearances.

As a result, the regular mechanisms (involving the vo'a and the go'a-series, explained in [Sec. 7.6](/en/books/cll/7/#sec-7-6) and [Sec. 7.8](/en/books/cll/7/#sec-7-8)) for referring to individual sumti of a bridi cannot refer to any of the trailing places of [5.79](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-79), because they are not really _sumti of the bridi_ at all.

When inverting a more complex tanru, it is possible to invert it only at the most general modifier-modified pair. The only possible inversion of [5.19](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-19), for instance, is:

<span id="ex-5-80"></span><span id="example-random-id-7uS2"></span>
> **ta nixli [bo] ckule co cmalu**
> _That (is-a-girl type-of school) of-type little._
> _That's a girls' school which is small._

Note that the **bo** of [5.19](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-19) is optional in [5.80](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-80), because **co** groups more loosely than any other cmavo used in tanru, including none at all. Not even **ke**…**ke'e** parentheses can encompass a **co**:

<span id="ex-5-81"></span><span id="example-random-id-y501"></span>
> **ta cmalu - ke nixli - ckule [ke'e] co melbi**
> _That is-a-(little type-of ( girl type-of school )) of-type pretty._
> _That's a small school for girls which is beautiful._

In [5.81](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-81), the **ke'e** is automatically inserted before the **co** rather than at its usual place at the end of the selbri. As a result, there is a simple and mechanical rule for removing **co** from any selbri: change _A co B_ to _ke B ke'e A_. (At the same time, any sumti following the selbri must be transformed into **be**…**bei**…**be'o** form and attached following B.) Therefore,

<span id="ex-5-82"></span><span id="example-random-id-4c0A"></span>
> **ckule co melbi nixli**
> _school of-type pretty girl_
> _school for beautiful girls_

means the same as:

<span id="ex-5-83"></span><span id="example-random-id-w5cI"></span>
> **ke melbi nixli ke'e ckule**
> _( pretty girl ) school_

Multiple **co** cmavo can appear within a selbri, indicating multiple inversions: a right-grouping rule is employed, as for **bo**. The above rule can be applied to interpret such selbri, but all **co** cmavo must be removed simultaneously:

<span id="ex-5-84"></span><span id="example-random-id-yLn5"></span>
> **ckule co nixli co cmalu**
> _school of-type (girl of-type little)_

becomes formally

<span id="ex-5-85"></span><span id="example-random-id-mM3Q"></span>
> **ke ke cmalu ke'e nixli ke'e ckule**
> _( ( little ) girl ) school_

which by the left-grouping rule is simply

<span id="ex-5-86"></span><span id="example-random-id-LGRz"></span>
> **cmalu nixli ckule**
> _little girl school_
> _school for little girls_

As stated above, the selbri places, other than the first, of

<span id="ex-5-87"></span><span id="example-random-id-Qcwd"></span>
> **mi klama co sutra**
> _I am-a-goer of-type quick_
> _I go quickly_

cannot be filled by placing sumti after the selbri, because any sumti in that position fill the places of **sutra**, the seltau. However, the tertau places (which means in effect the selbri places) can be filled with **be**:

<span id="ex-5-88"></span><span id="example-random-id-PDAh"></span>
> **mi klama be le zarci be'o co sutra**
> _I am-a-goer ( to-the store ) of-type quick._
> _I go to the store quickly._

So far we have only discussed brivla and tanru built up from brivla as possible selbri. In fact, there are a few other constructions in Lojban which are grammatically equivalent to brivla: they can be used either directly as selbri, or as components in tanru. Some of these types of simple selbri are discussed at length in [Ch. 7](/en/books/cll/7/#ch-7), [Ch. 11](/en/books/cll/11/#ch-11), and [Ch. 18](/en/books/cll/18/#ch-18); but for completeness these types are mentioned here with a brief explanation and an example of their use in selbri.

The cmavo of selma'o GOhA (with one exception) serve as pro-bridi, providing a reference to the content of other bridi; none of them has a fixed meaning. The most commonly used member of GOhA is probably **go'i**, which amounts to a repetition of the previous bridi, or part of it. If I say:

<span id="ex-5-89"></span><span id="example-random-id-2UvG"></span>
> **la djan. klama le zarci**
> _That-named John goes-to the market._

you may retort:

<span id="ex-5-90"></span><span id="example-random-id-EvoD"></span>
> **la djan. go'i troci**
> _That-named John [repeat-last] are-a-trier._
> _John tries to._

[5.90](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-90) is short for:

<span id="ex-5-91"></span><span id="example-random-id-nKQ3"></span>
> **la djan. klama be le zarci be'o - troci**
> _That-named John is-a-goer ( to-the market ) type-of trier._

because the whole bridi of [5.89](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-89) has been packaged up into the single word **go'i** and inserted into [5.90](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-90).

The exceptional member of GOhA is **du**, which represents the relation of identity. Its place structure is:

for as many places as are given. More information on selma'o GOhA is available in [Ch. 7](/en/books/cll/7/#ch-7).

Lojban mathematical expressions (mekso) can be incorporated into selbri in two different ways. Mathematical operators such as **su'i**, meaning _plus_, can be transformed into selbri by prefixing them with **nu'a** (of selma'o NUhA). The resulting place structure is:

for as many arguments as are required. (The result goes in the x1 place because the number of following places may be indefinite.) For example:

<span id="ex-5-92"></span><span id="example-random-id-pp6j"></span>
> **li vo nu'a--su'i li re li re**
> _The-number 4 is-the-sum-of the-number 2 and-the-number 2._

A possible tanru example might be:

<span id="ex-5-93"></span><span id="example-random-id-K7yz"></span>
> **mi jimpe tu'a loi nu'a--su'i nabmi**
> _I understand something-about the-mass-of is-the-sum-of problems._
> _I understand addition problems._

More usefully, it is possible to combine a mathematical expression with a cmavo of selma'o MOI to create one of various numerical selbri. Details are available in [Sec. 18.11](/en/books/cll/18/#sec-18-11). Here are a few tanru:

<span id="ex-5-94"></span><span id="example-random-id-qjWh"></span>
> **la prim. palvr. pamoi cusku**
> _That-named Preem Palver is-the-1-th speaker._
> _Preem Palver is the first speaker._

<span id="ex-5-95"></span><span id="example-random-id-qJwU"></span>
> **la an,iis. joi la .asun.**
> _That-named Anyi massed-with that-named Asun_

Finally, an important type of simple selbri which is not a brivla is the abstraction. Grammatically, abstractions are simple: a cmavo of selma'o NU, followed by a bridi, followed by the elidable terminator **kei** of selma'o KEI. Semantically, abstractions are an extremely subtle and powerful feature of Lojban whose full ramifications are documented in [Ch. 11](/en/books/cll/11/#ch-11). A few examples:

<span id="ex-5-96"></span><span id="example-random-id-5szz"></span>
> **ti nu zdile kei kumfa**
> _This is-an-event-of amusement - room._
> _This is an amusement room._

[5.96](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-96) is quite distinct in meaning from:

<span id="ex-5-97"></span><span id="example-random-id-yNSI"></span>
> **ti zdile kumfa**
> _This is-an-amuser room._

which suggests the meaning _a room that amuses someone_.

<span id="sec-5-10"></span><span id="section-me-selbri"></span>
### selbri based on sumti: me

The following cmavo are discussed in this section:

<table>
<tbody>
<tr>
<td>**me**</td>
<td>`ME`</td>
<td>changes sumti to simple selbri</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>**me'u**</td>
<td>`MEhU`</td>
<td>terminator for</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

A sumti can be made into a simple selbri by preceding it with **me** (of selma'o ME) and following it with the elidable terminator **me'u** (of selma'o MEhU). This makes a selbri with the place structure

which is true of the thing, or things, that are the referents of the sumti, and not of anything else. For example, consider the sumti

<span id="ex-5-98"></span><span id="example-random-id-v6QW"></span>
> **le ci nolraitru**
> _the three noblest-governors_
> _the three kings_

If these are understood to be the Three Kings of Christian tradition, who arrive every year on January 6, then we may say:

<span id="ex-5-99"></span><span id="example-random-id-99r3"></span>
> **la BALtazar. cu me le--ci--nolraitru**
> _That-named Balthazar - is-one-of-the-referents-of _the three kings.__
> _Balthazar is one of the three kings._

and likewise

<span id="ex-5-100"></span><span id="example-random-id-gPnL"></span>
> **la kaspar. cu me le ci nolraitru**
> _Caspar is one of the three kings._

and

<span id="ex-5-101"></span><span id="example-random-id-4827"></span>
> **la melxi,or. cu me le ci nolraitru**
> _Melchior is one of the three kings._

If the sumti refers to a single object, then the effect of **me** is much like that of **du**:

<span id="ex-5-102"></span><span id="example-random-id-HMHc"></span>
> **do du la djan.**
> _You are-identical-with that-named _John.__
> _You are John._

means the same as

<span id="ex-5-103"></span><span id="example-random-id-GMCc"></span>
> **do me la--djan.**
> _You are-the-referent-of _that-named John.__
> _You are John._

It is common to use **me** selbri, especially those based on name sumti using **la**, as seltau. For example:

<span id="ex-5-104"></span><span id="example-random-id-raQG"></span>
> **ta me lai--kraislr. [me'u] karce**
> _That (is-a-referent-of _the-mass-named Chrysler_) car._
> _That is a Chrysler car._

The elidable terminator **me'u** can usually be omitted. It is absolutely required only if the **me** selbri is being used in an indefinite description (a type of sumti explained in [Sec. 6.8](/en/books/cll/6/#sec-6-8)), and if the indefinite description is followed by a relative clause (explained in [Ch. 8](/en/books/cll/8/#ch-8)) or a sumti logical connective (explained in [Sec. 14.6](/en/books/cll/14/#sec-14-6)). Without a **me'u**, the relative clause or logical connective would appear to belong to the sumti embedded in the **me** expression. Here is a contrasting pair of sentences:

<span id="ex-5-105"></span><span id="example-random-id-qJyi"></span>
> **re me le ci nolraitru .e la djan. [me'u] cu blabi**
> _Two of the group _the three kings and John_ are white._

<span id="ex-5-106"></span><span id="example-random-id-qJyK"></span>
> **re me le ci nolraitru me'u .e la djan. cu blabi**
> _Two of the three kings, and John, are white._

In [5.105](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-105) the **me** selbri covers the three kings plus John, and the indefinite description picks out two of them that are said to be white: we cannot say which two. In [5.106](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-106), though, the **me** selbri covers only the three kings: two of them are said to be white, and so is John.

Finally, here is another example requiring **me'u**:

<span id="ex-5-107"></span><span id="example-random-id-ygzq"></span>
> **ta me--la'e--le--se--cusku--be--do me'u - cukta**
> _That is-a-(what-you-said) - type-of book._
> _That is the kind of book you were talking about._

There are other sentences where either **me'u** or some other elidable terminator must be expressed:

<span id="ex-5-108"></span><span id="example-random-id-8yDj"></span>
> **le me--le--ci--nolraitru [ku] me'u nunsalci**
> _the (the--three--kings) - - type-of-event-of-celebrating_
> _the Three Kings celebration_

requires either **ku** or **me'u** to be explicit, and (as with **be'o** in [Sec. 5.7](/en/books/cll/5/#sec-5-7)) the **me'u** leaves no doubt which cmavo it is paired with.

<span id="sec-5-11"></span><span id="section-place-conversion"></span>
### Conversion of simple selbri

Conversion is the process of changing a selbri so that its places appear in a different order. This is not the same as labeling the sumti with the cmavo of FA, as mentioned in [Sec. 5.7](/en/books/cll/5/#sec-5-7), and then rearranging the order in which the sumti are spoken or written. Conversion transforms the selbri into a distinct, though closely related, selbri with renumbered places.

In Lojban, conversion is accomplished by placing a cmavo of selma'o SE before the selbri:

<span id="ex-5-109"></span><span id="example-random-id-29Gu"></span>
> **mi prami do**
> _I love you._

is equivalent in meaning to:

<span id="ex-5-110"></span><span id="example-random-id-DWcN"></span>
> **do se prami mi**
> _You [swap--x1--and--x2] love me._
> _You are loved by me._

Conversion is fully explained in [Sec. 9.4](/en/books/cll/9/#sec-9-4). For the purposes of this chapter, the important point about conversion is that it applies only to the following simple selbri. When trying to convert a tanru, therefore, it is necessary to be careful! Consider [5.111](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-111):

<span id="ex-5-111"></span><span id="example-random-id-mPX8"></span>
> **la .alis. cu cadzu klama le zarci**
> _That-named Alice - is-a-walker type-of-goer-to the market._
> _Alice walks to the market._

To convert this sentence so that **le zarci** is in the x1 place, one correct way is:

<span id="ex-5-112"></span><span id="example-random-id-4mbn"></span>
> **le zarci cu se**
> _The market - is-a-[swap--x1/x2]_

The **ke**…**ke'e** brackets cause the entire tanru to be converted by the **se**, which would otherwise convert only **cadzu**, leading to:

<span id="ex-5-113"></span><span id="example-random-id-UNt0"></span>
> **le zarci cu se cadzu**
> _The market - (is-a-[swap--x1/x2] walker)_

whatever that might mean. An alternative approach, since the place structure of **cadzu klama** is that of **klama** alone, is to convert only the latter:

<span id="ex-5-114"></span><span id="example-random-id-U0fo"></span>
> **le zarci cu cadzu se--klama la .alis.**
> _The market - walkingly is-gone-to-by that-named Alice._

But the tanru in [5.114](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-114) may or may not have the same meaning as that in [5.111](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-111); in particular, because **cadzu** is not converted, there is a suggestion that although Alice is the goer, the market is the walker. With a different sumti as x1, this seemingly odd interpretation might make considerable sense:

<span id="ex-5-115"></span><span id="example-random-id-XEnd"></span>
> **la djan. cu cadzu se--klama la .alis**
> _That-named John - walkingly is-gone-to-by that-named Alice_

suggests that Alice is going to John, who is a moving target.

There is an alternative type of conversion, using the cmavo **jai** of selma'o JAI optionally followed by a modal or tense construction. Grammatically, such a combination behaves exactly like conversion using SE. More details can be found in [Sec. 9.12](/en/books/cll/9/#sec-9-12).

<span id="sec-5-12"></span><span id="section-selbri-scalar-negation"></span>
### Scalar negation of selbri

Negation is too large and complex a topic to explain fully in this chapter; see [Ch. 15](/en/books/cll/15/#ch-15). In brief, there are two main types of negation in Lojban. This section is concerned with so-called _scalar negation_, which is used to state that a true relation between the sumti is something other than what the selbri specifies. Scalar negation is expressed by cmavo of selma'o NAhE:

<span id="ex-5-116"></span><span id="example-random-id-4oxH"></span>
> **la .alis. cu na'e ke cadzu klama [ke'e] le zarci**
> _That-named Alice - non- ( walkingly goes-to ) the market._
> _Alice doesn't walk to the market._

meaning that Alice's relationship to the market is something other than that of walking there. But if the **ke** were omitted, the result would be:

<span id="ex-5-117"></span><span id="example-random-id-NL2Y"></span>
> **la .alis. cu na'e cadzu klama le zarci**
> _That-named Alice - non- walkingly goes-to the market._
> _Alice doesn't walk to the market._

meaning that Alice does go there in some way (**klama** is not negated), but by a means other than that of walking. [5.116](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-116) negates both **cadzu** and **klama**, suggesting that Alice's relation to the market is something different from walkingly-going; it might be walking without going, or going without walking, or neither.

Of course, any of the simple selbri types explained in [Sec. 5.9](/en/books/cll/5/#sec-5-9) may be used in place of brivla in any of these examples:

<span id="ex-5-118"></span><span id="example-random-id-YB00"></span>
> **la djonz. cu na'e pamoi cusku**
> _That-named Jones - is-non- 1st speaker_
> _Jones is not the first speaker._

Since only **pamoi** is negated, an appropriate inference is that he is some other kind of speaker.

Here is an assortment of more complex examples showing the interaction of scalar negation with normal grouping, **ke** and **ke'e** grouping, logical connection, and sumti linked with **be** and **bei**:

<span id="ex-5-119"></span><span id="example-random-id-I1Rd"></span>
> **mi na'e sutra cadzu be fi le birka be'o klama le zarci**
> _I ((non- quickly) (walking - using the arms )) go-to the market._
> _I go to the market, walking using my arms other than quickly._

In [5.119](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-119), **na'e** negates only **sutra**. Contrast [5.120](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-120):

<span id="ex-5-120"></span><span id="example-random-id-KcK8"></span>
> **mi na'e ke sutra cadzu be fi le birka [be'o]**
> _I non- ( quickly (walking - using the arms )_

Now consider [5.121](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-121) and [5.122](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-122), which are equivalent in meaning, but use normal grouping and **ke** grouping respectively:

<span id="ex-5-121"></span><span id="example-random-id-qjyW"></span>
> **mi sutra cadzu be fi le birka be'o**
> _I (quickly (walking - using the arms )_

<span id="ex-5-122"></span><span id="example-random-id-qjyy"></span>
> **mi ke sutra cadzu be fi le birka [be'o] ke'e**
> _I ( (quickly (walking - using the arms ) )_

However, if we place a **na'e** at the beginning of the selbri in both [5.121](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-121) and [5.122](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-122), we get different results:

<span id="ex-5-123"></span><span id="example-random-id-qjyz"></span>
> **mi na'e sutra cadzu be fi le birka be'o**
> _I ((non- quickly) (walking - using the arms )_

<span id="ex-5-124"></span><span id="example-random-id-qJzr"></span>
> **mi na'e ke sutra cadzu be fi le birka [be'o] ke'e**
> _I (non ( quickly (walking - using the arms) - )_

The difference arises because the **na'e** in [5.124](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-124) negates the whole construction from **ke** to **ke'e**, whereas in [5.123](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-123) it negates **sutra** alone.

Beware of omitting terminators in these complex examples! If the explicit **ke'e** is left out in [5.124](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-124), it is transformed into:

<span id="ex-5-125"></span><span id="example-random-id-Y53U"></span>
> **mi na'e ke sutra cadzu be fi le birka be'o**
> _I non- ( quickly ((walking - using the arms) )_

And if both **ke'e** and **be'o** are omitted, the results are even sillier:

<span id="ex-5-126"></span><span id="example-random-id-0WLq"></span>
> **mi na'e ke sutra cadzu be fi le birka je masno**
> _I non ( quickly walk - on-my (the arm-type and slow)_

In [5.126](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-126), everything after **be** is a linked sumti, so the place structure is that of **cadzu**, whose x2 place is the surface walked upon. It is less than clear what an _arm-type goer_ might be. Furthermore, since the x3 place has been occupied by the linked sumti, the **le zarci** following the selbri falls into the nonexistent x4 place of **cadzu**. As a result, the whole example, though grammatical, is complete nonsense. (The bracketed Lojban words appear where a fluent Lojbanist would understand them to be implied.)

Finally, it is also possible to place **na'e** before a **gu'e**…**gi** logically connected tanru construction. The meaning of this usage has not yet been firmly established.

<span id="sec-5-13"></span><span id="section-bridi-negation-tenses"></span>
### Tenses and bridi negation

A bridi can have cmavo associated with it which specify the time, place, or mode of action. For example, in

<span id="ex-5-127"></span><span id="example-random-id-uz13"></span>
> **mi pu klama le zarci**
> _I [past] go-to the market._
> _I went to the market._

the cmavo **pu** specifies that the action of the speaker going to the market takes place in the past. Tenses are explained in full detail in [Ch. 10](/en/books/cll/10/#ch-10). Tense is semantically a property of the entire bridi; however, the usual syntax for tenses attaches them at the front of the selbri, as in [5.127](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-127). There are alternative ways of expressing tense information as well. Modals, which are explained in [Ch. 9](/en/books/cll/9/#ch-9), behave in the same way as tenses.

Similarly, a bridi may have the particle **na** (of selma'o NA) attached to the beginning of the selbri to negate the bridi. A negated bridi expresses what is false without saying anything about what is true. Do not confuse this usage with the scalar negation of [Sec. 5.12](/en/books/cll/5/#sec-5-12). For example:

<span id="ex-5-128"></span><span id="example-random-id-PYSP"></span>
> **la djonz. na pamoi cusku**
> _That-named Jones (Not!) is-the-first speaker_
> _It is not true that Jones is the first speaker._

Jones may be the second speaker, or not a speaker at all; [5.128](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-128) doesn't say. There are other ways of expressing bridi negation as well; the topic is explained fully in [Ch. 15](/en/books/cll/15/#ch-15).

Various combinations of tense and bridi negation cmavo are permitted. If both are expressed, either order is permissible with no change in meaning:

<span id="ex-5-129"></span><span id="example-random-id-RV4C"></span>
> **mi na pu klama le zarci**
> _It is false that I went to the market._

It is also possible to have more than one **na**, in which case pairs of **na** cmavo cancel out:

<span id="ex-5-130"></span><span id="example-random-id-TuP7"></span>
> **mi na na klama le zarci**
> _It is false that it is false that I go to the market._

It is even possible, though somewhat pointless, to have multiple **na** cmavo and tense cmavo mixed together, subject to the limitation that two adjacent tense cmavo will be understood as a compound tense, and must fit the grammar of tenses as explained in [Ch. 10](/en/books/cll/10/#ch-10).

<span id="ex-5-131"></span><span id="example-random-id-hw6g"></span>
> **mi na pu na ca klama le zarci**
> _I [not] [past] [not] [present] go-to the market_
> _It is not the case that in the past it was not the case that in the present I went to the market._

Tense, modal, and negation cmavo can appear only at the beginning of the selbri. They cannot be embedded within it.

<span id="sec-5-16"></span><span id="section-pretty-school-groupings"></span>
### Pretty little girls' school: forty ways to say it

The following examples show every possible grouping arrangement of **melbi cmalu nixli ckule** using **bo** or **ke**…**ke'e** for grouping and **je** or **je****bo** for logical connection. Most of these are definitely not plausible interpretations of the English phrase _pretty little girls' school_, especially those which describe something which is both a girl and a school.

[5.26](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-26), [5.27](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-27), [5.28](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-28), [5.29](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-29), and [5.36](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-36) are repeated here as [5.132](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-132), [5.140](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-140), [5.148](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-148), [5.156](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-156), and [5.164](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-164) respectively. The seven examples following each of these share the same grouping pattern, but differ in the presence or absence of **je** at each possible site. Some of the examples have more than one Lojban version. In that case, they differ only in grouping mechanism, and are always equivalent in meaning.

The logical connective **je** is associative: that is, _A and (B and C)_ is the same as _(A and B) and C_. Therefore, some of the examples have the same meaning as others. In particular, [5.139](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-139), [5.147](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-147), [5.155](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-155), [5.163](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-163), and [5.171](/en/books/cll/5/#ex-5-171) all have the same meaning because all four brivla are logically connected and the grouping is simply irrelevant. Other equivalent forms are noted in the examples themselves. However, if **je** were replaced by **naja** or **jo** or most of the other logical connectives, the meanings would become distinct.

It must be emphasized that, because of the ambiguity of all tanru, the English translations are by no means definitive – they represent only one possible interpretation of the corresponding Lojban sentence.
