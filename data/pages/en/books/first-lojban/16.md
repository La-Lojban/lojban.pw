## Lesson 16. Negation

<div class="note">

You may notice more vocabulary per chapter from here on: the **skeleton** of Lojban is mostly set, so we widen expression and reading practice.

</div>

<speaker sprite="koc1">We’ve had a lot of plain **declaratives** — so let’s tackle **negation**.</speaker>

The simplest pattern: put **na** immediately **before the selbri** — “it’s not the case that …”

<dl class="drani">
<dt>na</dt>
<dd>negates the bridi; typically immediately before the selbri.</dd>
</dl>

> mi **na** catra le gerku
> _I am not killing the dog._

> la .miran. **na** dasni lo crino kosta
> _Milan is not wearing a green coat._

> mi **na** du la .kocon.
> _I am not Koshon._

<dl class="valsi">
<dt>catra</dt>
<dd>x1 kills x2 by method x3</dd>
<dt>gerku</dt>
<dd>x1 is a dog of species/breed x2</dd>
<dt>dasni</dt>
<dd>x1 wears x2 as garment x3</dd>
<dt>crino</dt>
<dd>x1 is green</dd>
<dt>kosta</dt>
<dd>x1 is a coat of material x2</dd>
</dl>

<speaker sprite="koc1">Like **tense tags**, **na** before the selbri also marks where the selbri starts — it can stand in for **cu**.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor1">That’s almost too plain to comment on… Oh — is there a word for strong **“it really is true”** affirmation?</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc2">**ja'a** — it works like **na**, but **affirms the whole bridi**.</speaker>

<dl class="drani">
<dt>ja'a</dt>
<dd>emphatic affirmation before selbri.</dd>
</dl>

> do **ja'a** ca jmive
> _You **are** alive now._

> ti **ja'a** prenu .i na minji
> _This **is** a person, not a machine._

<dl class="valsi">
<dt>prenu</dt>
<dd>x1 is a person (psychological sense)</dd>
<dt>minji</dt>
<dd>x1 is a machine for purpose x2</dd>
</dl>

<speaker sprite="sor2">Nice — paired particles that work the same way.</speaker>

<hr />

<speaker sprite="koc1">So **na** / **ja'a** negate or affirm the **whole bridi**. **NAhE** cmavo instead tweak the **selbri** (same structural “slot” idea as **SE**).</speaker>

**They build new predicate senses**: opposite, midpoint, emphatic same-sense, or “other than the usual reading.”

<dl class="drani">
<dt>to'e</dt>
<dd>opposite polar meaning</dd>
<dt>no'e</dt>
<dd>neutral midpoint</dd>
<dt>je'a</dt>
<dd>same meaning, emphatic (“really is …”)</dd>
<dt>na'e</dt>
<dd>“other than the usual sense of …” (non-specific contrary)</dd>
</dl>

> mi prami do / mi **to'e prami** do
> _love / “anti-love” (hate-ish)._

> lo vi tanxe cu barda / lo vi tanxe cu **no'e barda**
> _big / neither big nor small._

> lo nu cilre fi lo lojbo gerna cu **je'a zdile** mi
> _Learning Lojban grammar is **really** fun._

<dl class="valsi">
<dt>prami</dt>
<dd>x1 loves x2</dd>
<dt>tanxe</dt>
<dd>x1 is a box for contents x2, material x3</dd>
<dt>barda</dt>
<dd>x1 is big in property x2 compared to x3</dd>
<dt>cilre</dt>
<dd>x1 learns fact x2 about subject x3 from source x4 by method x5</dd>
<dt>gerna</dt>
<dd>x1 is the grammar of language x2 regarding property x3</dd>
<dt>zdile</dt>
<dd>x1 is fun for x2 in aspect x3</dd>
</dl>

<speaker sprite="koc1">**NAhE** cannot replace **cu** — the **NAhE + selbri** chunk can still form **tanru** pieces.</speaker>

> lo crino **to'e barda**
> _green small-ish thing_

> lo **na'e crino** kosta
> _a non-green coat_

<speaker sprite="sor5">From the definitions alone, **na** and **na'e** don’t look *that* different…</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc1">**na** negates the **whole claim**. **na'e** builds a **different predicate** (“not green” still **suggests** colour — “suggestive” negation).</speaker>

> la .miran. **na** dasni lo crino kosta
> _It’s false that Milan wears a green coat (maybe naked, maybe other coat)._

> la .miran. cu dasni lo **na'e crino** kosta
> _Milan wears a **non-green** coat._

<speaker sprite="sor2">**ti na nanmu** vs **ti na'e nanmu** — first: not a man (could be a rock); second: suggests **some** gender, not male.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc5">In practice **na** is the safe default; use **NAhE** when you want the narrower “other kind of …” nuance.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor7">Default to **na**; reach for **NAhE** when you want to be precise.</speaker>
