## Lesson 16. Negation

<div class="note">

You may notice more vocabulary per chapter from here on: the _skeleton_ of Lojban is mostly set, so we widen expression and reading practice.

</div>

<speaker sprite="koc1">Up until now, we've mostly dealt with straightforward _statements_. But what if you want to say that something *isn't* the case? It's time to tackle _negation_.

The simplest way is to place the word **na** immediately before the _selbri_. This basically says, "It is not true that..."</speaker>

<dl class="drani">
<dt>na</dt>
<dd>negates the bridi; typically immediately before the selbri.</dd>
</dl>

> **mi na catra le gerku**
> _I am not killing the dog._

> **la .miran. na dasni lo crino kosta**
> _Milan is not wearing a green coat._

> **mi na du la .kocon.**
> _I am not Koshon._

<dl class="valsi">
<dt>catra</dt>
<dd>x<sub>1</sub> kills x<sub>2</sub> by method x<sub>3</sub></dd>
<dt>gerku</dt>
<dd>x<sub>1</sub> is a dog of species/breed x<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dt>dasni</dt>
<dd>x<sub>1</sub> wears x<sub>2</sub> as garment x<sub>3</sub></dd>
<dt>crino</dt>
<dd>x<sub>1</sub> is green</dd>
<dt>kosta</dt>
<dd>x<sub>1</sub> is a coat of material x<sub>2</sub></dd>
</dl>

<speaker sprite="koc2">Just like the tense tags we saw earlier, placing **na** before the selbri also clearly marks where the verb begins. It can even take the place of **cu**.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor1">That's remarkably simple! Is there an opposite word? Like a way to say, "Yes, it really *is* true!"</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc5">There certainly is! That would be **ja'a**. It works exactly like **na**, but it **affirms** the entire sentence instead of negating it.</speaker>

<dl class="drani">
<dt>ja'a</dt>
<dd>emphatic affirmation before selbri.</dd>
</dl>

> **do ja'a ca jmive**
> _You **are** (really) alive right now._

> **ti ja'a prenu .i na minji**
> _This **is** a person; it's not a machine._

<dl class="valsi">
<dt>prenu</dt>
<dd>x<sub>1</sub> is a person (psychological sense)</dd>
<dt>minji</dt>
<dd>x<sub>1</sub> is a machine for purpose x<sub>2</sub></dd>
</dl>

<speaker sprite="sor7">Nice — paired particles that work the same way.</speaker>

---

<speaker sprite="koc2">So, **na** and **ja'a** deal with the truth of the _entire sentence_. But we also have the _NAhE_-series of words, which allow you to tweak the meaning of the _selbri_ itself (similar to how _SE_ works).

These words let you build <u>new shades of meaning</u>: the opposite, a neutral midpoint, or just something "other than" the standard definition.</speaker>

<dl class="drani">
<dt>to'e</dt><dd>the logical _opposite_ (polar meaning)</dd>
<dt>no'e</dt><dd>the _neutral midpoint_ (not X, not its opposite)</dd>
<dt>je'a</dt><dd>the _strong affirmation_ ("it really is [_selbri_]")</dd>
<dt>na'e</dt><dd>_something else_ ("other than the usual sense of [_selbri_]")</dd>
</dl>

> **mi prami do / mi to'e prami do**
> _I love you / I hate you (the opposite of love)._

> **lo vi tanxe cu barda / lo vi tanxe cu no'e barda**
> _The box is big / The box is medium-sized (not big, not small)._

> **lo nu cilre fi lo lojbo gerna cu je'a zdile mi**
> _Learning Lojban grammar <u>really is</u> fun for me._

<dl class="valsi">
<dt>prami</dt>
<dd>x<sub>1</sub> loves x<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dt>tanxe</dt>
<dd>x<sub>1</sub> is a box for contents x<sub>2</sub>, material x<sub>3</sub></dd>
<dt>barda</dt>
<dd>x<sub>1</sub> is big in property x<sub>2</sub> compared to x<sub>3</sub></dd>
<dt>cilre</dt>
<dd>x<sub>1</sub> learns fact x<sub>2</sub> about subject x<sub>3</sub> from source x<sub>4</sub> by method x<sub>5</sub></dd>
<dt>gerna</dt>
<dd>x<sub>1</sub> is the grammar of language x<sub>2</sub> regarding property x<sub>3</sub></dd>
<dt>zdile</dt>
<dd>x<sub>1</sub> is fun for x<sub>2</sub> in aspect x<sub>3</sub></dd>
</dl>

<speaker sprite="koc2">Keep in mind that _NAhE_ words don't act as sentence markers like **na** or **cu** do. The _[NAhE] + selbri_ combination is still treated as a single unit and can still form part of a _tanru_.</speaker>

> **lo crino to'e barda**
> _green small-ish thing_

> **lo na'e crino kosta**
> _a non-green coat (a coat of some other color)_

<speaker sprite="sor5">From the definitions alone, **na** and **na'e** don’t look *that* different…</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc2">**na** negates the _entire claim_ (it says the whole sentence is false). **na'e** simply points to a _different predicate_ ("not green" still implies we're talking about a color—it's what we call "suggestive negation").</speaker>

> **la .miran. na dasni lo crino kosta**
> _It’s false that Milan wears a green coat (maybe naked, maybe other coat)._

> **la .miran. cu dasni lo na'e crino kosta**
> _Milan is wearing a coat that is something other than green._

<speaker sprite="sor7">Oh, I see. So **ti na nanmu** means "This thing is not a man" (it could be a rock for all we know). But **ti na'e nanmu** suggests it's *something like* a man or has a gender, but specifically is *not* a man.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc5">In general, **na** is your safe default for negation. Use the **NAhE** series when you want that specific "it's another kind of..." nuance.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor7">Negate with **na**, refine with **NAhE**. Simple!</speaker>
