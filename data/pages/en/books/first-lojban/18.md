## Lesson 18. Commands and requests

<speaker sprite="koc2">Now let's look at how to _urge_ someone to do something: _commands_.

To make an imperative sentence, you just _replace "do" with the word_ **ko**.</speaker>

ko
: The imperative "you"; it covers a wide range from strict commands to polite requests.


> **ko stali ta**
> _Stay there._

> **ko na kusru mi**
> _Don’t be mean to me._

> **lo prenu cu prami ko**
> _Be someone that people love._


<speaker sprite="sor2">So putting **na** before the _selbri_ with **ko** means "don't..." That last example is interesting—it takes a moment to wrap my head around it.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc2">**ko** can fill any **terbricmi** — the command is “make this _bridi_ true.”</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor7">So instead of ordering someone to love you, **lo prenu cu prami ko** is more like "Behave in a way that *results* in people loving you."</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc2">**ko** alone already spans “do it” through “please do”; that covers most imperative needs.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc2">If you want to add more nuance—like making a polite request, a suggestion, or granting permission—you can use _attitudinals_ at the start of your sentence:</speaker>

.e'o
: request

.e'u
: suggestion

.e'unai
: warning against

.e'a
: permission

.e'anai
: forbiddance

.e'i
: constraint / obligation

.e'inai
: release / “up to you”

<speaker sprite="koc2">(Plus **pei** for “may I?” patterns — see below.)</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor2">Some sentences keep **do** instead of **ko** — proposals and permissions aren’t always _straight imperatives_.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc2">We’ll go deeper into the **nai** suffix in the [finale](/en/books/first-lojban/#lesson-25-attitudinals-ui). But generally, if it's a suggestion or a request rather than a direct order, it's very common to use **do** instead of **ko**.

You can also use the word **pei** to turn an _attitudinal_ into a question:</speaker>

pei
: Turns the preceding attitudinal word into a question.


> **.e'a pei mi lasna do lo bitmu lo dunja badna**
> _May I stick you to the wall with a frozen banana?_

> **― nai**
> _No._


<speaker sprite="sor2">I see. So **nai** flips the attitude, similar to how **to'e** flips a brivla. This is getting deep!</speaker>
