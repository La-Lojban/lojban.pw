## Lesson 18. Commands and requests

<speaker sprite="koc2">After negation and questions: how to **urge** someone to act — **commands**.

**Replace sumti _do_ with _ko_** in the bridi you want to be imperative.</speaker>

<dl class="drani">
<dt>ko</dt>
<dd>imperative <em>do</em>; wide politeness range from “do it” to “please do.”</dd>
</dl>


> **ko** stali ta
> _Stay there._

> **ko** na kusru mi
> _Don’t be mean to me._

> lo prenu cu prami **ko**
> _Act so that people love you._


<speaker sprite="sor1">So **na** in the **do** slot becomes **ko** for “don’t …” The last example takes a beat to see.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc1">**ko** can sit in any sumti slot — the command is “make this bridi true.”</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor2">So **lo prenu cu prami ko** is “behave so that people love you” — not a literal “love you” order.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc5">**ko** alone already spans “do it” through “please do”; that covers most imperative needs.</speaker>

---

<speaker sprite="koc1">For nuance (request, suggestion, permission, …) use **attitudinals** at **.i**-position:</speaker>

<dl class="drani">
<dt>.e'o</dt>
<dd>request</dd>
<dt>.e'u</dt>
<dd>suggestion</dd>
<dt>.e'unai</dt>
<dd>warning against</dd>
<dt>.e'a</dt>
<dd>permission</dd>
<dt>.e'anai</dt>
<dd>forbiddance</dd>
<dt>.e'i</dt>
<dd>constraint / obligation</dd>
<dt>.e'inai</dt>
<dd>release / “up to you”</dd>
</dl>


(Plus **pei** for “may I?” patterns — see below.)

<speaker sprite="sor5">Some sentences keep **do** instead of **ko** — proposals and permissions aren’t always straight imperatives.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc4">We’ll unpack **nai** on attitudinals more in the finale. When you’re not really commanding, **do** instead of **ko** is normal.

**Attitudinal + pei** asks whether the hearer holds that attitude:</speaker>

<dl class="drani">
<dt>pei</dt>
<dd>attitudinal question.</dd>
</dl>


> .e'a pei mi lasna do lo bitmu lo dunja badna
> _May I stick you to the wall with a frozen banana?_

> ― nai
> _No._


<speaker sprite="sor2">**nai** flips attitudinals — like **to'e** for NAhE, but not identical (details later).</speaker>
