## Lesson 33. What predicates really mean — and **zo'u** as a topic marker

<speaker sprite="sor1">Can I ask something I've been wondering about for a while? Those "x₁ loves x₂" definitions — they're in English. But Lojban isn't English. How do I know the English gloss captures the real meaning?</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sev2">You don't. Not completely. And that's worth talking about.</speaker>

### The limits of "hole-filling" definitions

<speaker sprite="sev1">When you look up a brivla, you see something like:

prami
: x<sub>1</sub> loves x<sub>2</sub>

That "x₁ loves x₂" is a _translation_ — a Japanese, English, or Russian window into a Lojban concept. Every translation carries the source language's baggage:

- English "loves" has tense built in (present tense default); Lojban **prami** doesn't care about tense.
- English separates "love" from "like" culturally; Lojban speakers use **prami** more broadly.
- English "love" is a verb — implying a doer and a receiver in a specific power arrangement; Lojban relations are more neutral.

</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor2">So the definition is a _hint_, not a theorem.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sev3">Right. The real meaning of a brivla is the _relation_ it names — a pattern in the world — not the English words used to describe it. When you internalize a brivla, you eventually stop translating and just know the shape of what it connects.

Think of it this way: **citka** names the "eating" relation. Whether that's "eating", "consume", "to eat", "eats" doesn't matter. The relation connects eater and food. _That's_ the concept.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor5">So "minimum description, maximum interpretation" applies to the definitions themselves too, not just the sentences.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sev2">Exactly. The place structure tells you _what kinds of things are involved_, not exactly how to translate every nuance into your native language.</speaker>

### Place structure: why those slots?

<speaker sprite="sev1">Each brivla has its slots because _that kind of situation usually involves those participants_. A "buying" relation (**pleji**, **vecnu**) typically involves a buyer, a seller, a thing sold, and a price — so those are the places. A "waiting" relation (**denpa**) involves a waiter, an event waited for, a state while waiting, and a trigger — so those are its places.

Less-central participants don't get core slots — they get tags (**ba**, **fi'o**, etc.). The design choice is: put the _most essential_ participants in the numbered slots, leave the rest for tags.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor1">And you never have to fill every slot. You say what you mean and let context fill the rest.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sev4">Lojban's motto, really: _minimum necessary statement, maximum interpretive latitude_. Don't say more than you mean.</speaker>

### zo'u — more than a prenex marker

<speaker sprite="sev1">Back in Lesson 28, you used **zo'u** to write the prenex of an existential statement. But **zo'u** has a second job: it's a **topic marker**.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor2">Topic as in… what the sentence is _about_?</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sev4">Yes. In most languages, topics and grammatical subjects often coincide, but they're different things. The grammatical x₁ slot in Lojban is whoever fills the first place of the predicate — that might not be what the speaker considers the "topic."

Anything placed before **zo'u** (any sumti, with or without tags) is marked as the topic of the following sentence.</speaker>

> **lo xanto zo'u lo nazbi cu clani**
> _As for elephants, the nose is long._ (Topic: elephants. Subject: the nose.)

> **ca lo bavlamdei zo'u mi cliva ti**
> _Tomorrow, I'm leaving here._ (Topic: tomorrow. Subject: I.)

xanto
: x<sub>1</sub> is an elephant/proboscid of species x<sub>2</sub>

nazbi
: x<sub>1</sub> is the nose of body x<sub>2</sub>, nasal passage x<sub>3</sub>

clani
: x<sub>1</sub> is long in dimension x<sub>2</sub>, by standard x<sub>3</sub>

<speaker sprite="sor1">So the elephant sentence — x₁ of **clani** is the _nose_, not the elephant, but the elephant is what we're talking about. The topic sits before **zo'u**, and then the normal predication follows.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sev2">Exactly. Compare the same content without explicit topic:</speaker>

> **lo nazbi be lo xanto cu clani**
> _The nose of an elephant is long._

<speaker sprite="sev4">Grammatically equivalent claim, but the topic/emphasis is different. The **zo'u** version says: "Here's what we're talking about (elephants), and here's what I'm saying about it (the nose is long)."

In practice, modern Lojban speakers often omit explicit topics and let context carry the weight. But **zo'u** is there whenever you want to be precise about what the sentence is _about_, independently of what fills x₁.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor7">So **zo'u** is doing double duty: prenex-separator in Lesson 28, topic-marker here. Same word.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sev3">Same word, same mechanism — you're always separating a "preamble" from the "main claim." In the prenex case, the preamble introduces quantified variables. In the topic case, it introduces what we're discussing. Both are just different kinds of "setup before the sentence proper."</speaker>
