## Lesson 28. (Extra) Existence and quantifiers

<pixra url="/assets/pixra/books/first-lojban/icons/koc1.png" caption="Koshon" definition=""></pixra>

**Existential claims** depend on **domain of discourse** — “two yellow things” may be true **inside the situation we’re talking about** (a picture, a story scene, a math diagram) but false if you silently widen the domain to **everything in the universe**. When you read a quantified sentence, ask: *counted within what frame?*

<speaker sprite="sor2">So “there is a bird” usually means **in what we can see**, not **in the whole universe**.</speaker>
### Quantifier prefix + **zo'u**

Template (logic-flavoured):

**[quantifiers + bound variables] zo'u [open sentence with those variables]**

<dl class="drani">
<dt>da, de, di</dt>
<dd>bound variables (default existential <strong>su'o pa</strong> if no explicit quantifier)</dd>
<dt>zo'u</dt>
<dd>separates prenex from bridi</dd>
</dl>


> su'o pa da zo'u da cukla
> _Something is round._

> re da zo'u da pelxu
> _Exactly two things are yellow._


Order in the prenex **matters** — **pa da re de** vs **re de pa da** are not the same claim.

### Dropping the prenex

Sometimes the prenex can be **folded** into the bridi in standard orders (see references).

**Scope picture (text only):** variables introduced in the prenex “bind” through the open sentence after **zo'u**. If you reorder quantifiers, you change **who exists depending on whom** — e.g. “for each person, there is a hat” is not the same as “there is one hat for everyone.” The formal details are a whole textbook chapter; here, remember **order in the prenex matters** and **domain of discourse** fixes what “there is” can see.
