The period **.** and comma **,** are punctuation (pause / syllable break), not letters in the same sense as the row above.

<speaker sprite="sor1">Hmm. Basically Roman-letter values, and no H, Q, or W.</speaker>
The tricky bits are **c (sh)**, **x (velar fricative)**, and **y (schwa)**? What exactly is “x”?

<speaker sprite="koc1">**x** is a voiceless velar fricative — between “k” and “h”. If you want more detail, see [Lojban pronunciation (Wikibooks)](https://en.wikibooks.org/wiki/Lojban/Pronunciation) or [ko lojbo .iu — pronunciation of c, k, x](http://guskant.github.io/kolojbo.iu/html/ch08s04.html) (with audio).</speaker>
<speaker sprite="sor2">(What was that again…?) I’ll listen before bed. And **y** is…?</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc2">That’s the **neutral vowel** — English **schwa**. Relax your mouth and voice; you’ll get something like it.</speaker>
<speaker sprite="sor1">Got it. How do you *name* the letters? Ay, bee, cee?</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc1">Consonants: **consonant + y + .**  
Vowels: **. + vowel + bu**</speaker>

> "a"
>
> _.abu_

> "o"
>
> _.obu_

> "b"
>
> _by._

> "s"
>
> _sy._

> "n"
>
> _ny._


The **.** is silence — a short pause or glottal stop. It is **not** a sentence-ending period in the English sense (we’ll get to that later). The dots at the edges of letter names are there so words don’t accidentally run together.

Also: the **apostrophe** appears **only between vowels**. Sequences like **k'a** or **d'e** are impossible. The apostrophe sounds like **h**, but Lojban treats it as a special sound, **not** as a consonant in the same sense as **b**, **m**, etc.

<speaker sprite="sor2">Okay, okay. **ts** and **tc** as affricates make sense; **dz** and **dj** as the voiced versions too.</speaker>

## Semivowels and diphthongs

<speaker sprite="koc1">From the chart you mostly have what you need; just learn how **vowel clusters** work.</speaker>

- **i + vowel** → y-glide + vowel  
- **u + vowel** → w-glide + vowel  

So in clusters, **i** and **u** often act as **semivowels**.

<speaker sprite="sor1">So **ies** is like English “yes”.</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc2">Right. Then there are **diphthongs**:</speaker>

- **au** — the **u** acts like a w-glide onset  
- **ei, ai, oi** — the **i** acts like a y-glide onset  

Those are read as **one syllable**.

<speaker sprite="sor3">What about **ao** or **ea**?</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc1">Those are **not** diphthongs in Lojban. Only those four count. If you string **a, e, o** together, slip **i**, **u**, or **'** between them.</speaker>
<speaker sprite="sor2">Fair enough.</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc5">Quiz time! Try reading these sentences aloud.</speaker>

### Exercise

- Read the following Lojban aloud.
  1. **coi ro do mi'e .soran. co'o .kocon.**  
     — Hello everyone; I’m Sora; goodbye, Koshon.
  1. **mi prami do .i semu'ibo dunda lo melbi xrula**  
     — I love you, so I give a beautiful flower.
  1. **.o'i mu xagji sofybakni cu zvati le purdi**  
     — (Watch out) five hungry Soviet cows are in that garden.

<speaker sprite="sor7">Let’s see…</speaker>

1. shoy… ro… do… mihe… soran… shoho… koshon (something like that)  
2. mi prami do… isemoohibo… dunda lo melbi hhrula  
3. oh-hee… mu… hagji… sofybakni… zvati le purdi  

How’s that?

· · · · ·

<speaker sprite="sor3">“Soviet cows” — what even *is* that?</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc1">The third one is a famous **pangram**: it uses **every Lojban letter**.</speaker>
<speaker sprite="sor2">Whoa, you’re right!</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc5">Fun, so I made one too — mine also has diphthongs, semivowels, and the affricates **ts, tc, dz, dj**!</speaker>

> **.ua ja'o le mu tsali dzena cu djicai lo nu re xagji sofybakni cu zvati tu noi tcadu vau .iepei**
>
> _“Ah — so those five strong seniors *really want* two hungry Soviet cows to be over there in that town, huh?”_

<speaker sprite="sor1">That’s *long*.</speaker>
