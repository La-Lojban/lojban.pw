The **.** represents silence—a brief pause or a glottal stop. Keep in mind it is _not_ a sentence-ending period like in English (we’ll cover those later). Dots are used in letter names to ensure words don’t accidentally blend together.

Also, a crucial rule: the **apostrophe** only ever appears _between two vowels_. You'll never see sequences like **k'a** or **d'e**. While the apostrophe sounds like a soft **h**, Lojban treats it as a special glide, _not_ as a standard consonant like **b** or **m**.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor7">Makes sense. **ts** and **tc** are like 'tz' and 'ch', and **dz** and **dj** are just their voiced counterparts. Simple enough!</speaker>

### Semivowels and diphthongs

<speaker sprite="koc1">The chart covers most of it, but you should also know how _vowel clusters_ work.

- **i + vowel** → like a 'y' sound + vowel (e.g., *ia* sounds like "ya")
- **u + vowel** → like a 'w' sound + vowel (e.g., *ua* sounds like "wa")

In these clusters, **i** and **u** act as _semivowels_.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor1">So **ies** is like English “yes”.</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc2">Exactly. Then we have **diphthongs**:

- **au** — sounds like "ow" in "cow"
- **ei, ai, oi** — like in "hey", "high", and "boy"

These are always treated as _a single syllable_.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor1">What about **ao** or **ea**?</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc1">Lojban only recognizes those four as diphthongs. If you want to connect **a, e, or o** directly, you have to separate them with **i**, **u**, or an **'** (apostrophe).</speaker>
<speaker sprite="sor7">Fair enough.</speaker>

### Exercise

- Read the following Lojban aloud.
  1. **coi ro do mi'e .soran. co'o .kocon.**  
     — Hello everyone; I’m Sora; goodbye, Koshon.
  1. **mi prami do .i semu'ibo dunda lo melbi xrula**  
     — I love you, so I give a beautiful flower.
  1. **.o'i mu xagji sofybakni cu zvati le purdi**  
     — (Watch out) five hungry Soviet cows are in that garden.
