## Lesson 4. Basic sentence structure

<speaker sprite="sor3">First the sounds, then the word types... Alright, Koshon, lay that Lojban grammar on me! I'm ready for the "Super Grammar"!</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc1">Let's start with basic _statements_. Think of a sentence as saying:

<font size="+2">Who or what / does what / is like what / to whom / where...</font>

Basically, you have _"the thing we're talking about"_ and _"what we're saying about it."_</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor1">Makes sense. Like "I eat this"—_I_ and _this_ are the "things," and _eat_ is the "doing."</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc5">Exactly. Borrowing terms from logic, we call the "doing/being" part the _predicate_ (_selbri_), and the terms that fill its roles _arguments_. In Lojban those argument terms are **sumti**; the numbered roles **x<sub>1</sub>**, **x<sub>2</sub>**, … are **terbricmi**, and their ordered sequence is the **terbri** (place structure). A full claim is a **bridi**.</speaker>

### Exercise

- In each English sentence, pick out the predicate and its arguments (in Lojban terms: the **selbri** and the **sumti**):
  1. That bird is blue.
  1. The man is drinking at the bar.
  1. This person is a woman.

<speaker sprite="sor2">Let me see... A **bridi** is built from a **selbri** and the list of **terbricmi** (slots) that are fulled with **sumti**. The **selbri** is the _is/does_ part, and the **sumti** are the _who/what_ terms going into those slots. Right?</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc5">Spot on.</speaker>
<speaker sprite="sor1">Okay, next.</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc2">Every Lojban predicate has a **terbri**—an ordered list of **terbricmi** (place slots) labeled **x<sub>1</sub>, x<sub>2</sub>, x<sub>3</sub>, ...**:

- **x<sub>1</sub>** is / does ...
- **x<sub>1</sub>** does **x<sub>2</sub>** ...
- **x<sub>1</sub>** is **x<sub>2</sub>**-ish to **x<sub>3</sub>** ...
- and so on.

Think of **x<sub>1</sub>, x<sub>2</sub>, etc.** as numbered "holes" (**terbricmi**) that you plug **sumti** into.

To make a sentence, just put the _sumti_ in their numbered order and tuck the _selbri_ in the middle. Simple, right?</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor1">Like:

**(sumti for x<sub>1</sub>) [selbri] (sumti for x<sub>2</sub>) (sumti for x<sub>3</sub>) …**</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor2">Wait, where *exactly* does the selbri go?</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc2">Strictly speaking, the _selbri_ can go _almost anywhere_—but most people put it _between_ the **sumti** for **x<sub>1</sub>** and the **sumti** for **x<sub>2</sub>**, much like English _SVO_ (_Subject-Verb-Object_).</speaker>
<speaker sprite="sor7">That feels pretty natural.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc2">Right — a familiar default. Here are some **sumti** you can use already:

mi
: I / me

do
: you

zo'e
: something unspecified / “whatever”

ti
: this

ta
: that (near you)

tu
: that (yonder)

ra
: he / she / it / they (in context)

Some predicates:

ninmu
: x<sub>1</sub> is a woman

citka
: x<sub>1</sub> eats x<sub>2</sub>

zunle
: x<sub>1</sub> is to the left of x<sub>2</sub> from viewpoint x<sub>3</sub>

sanli
: x<sub>1</sub> stands on x<sub>2</sub> using legs/support x<sub>3</sub>

nelci
: x<sub>1</sub> likes x<sub>2</sub>

</speaker>

### Exercise

- Translate into Lojban:
  1. I am a woman.
  1. You eat this.
  1. She is to the left of that (from my perspective).
  1. He stands there (with some legs/support).
  1. I like you.

<speaker sprite="sor1">So, line up the sumti, tuck in the selbri...

1. **mi ninmu**
2. **do citka ti**
3. **ra zunle tu mi**
4. **ra sanli tu zo'e**
5. **mi nelci do**

</speaker>
