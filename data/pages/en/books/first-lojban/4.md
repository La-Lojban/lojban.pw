## Lesson 4. Basic sentence structure

<speaker sprite="sor2">We’ve done sounds and word classes — bring on Lojban grammar! “Super grammar” — I’m ready!</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc1">Let’s start with **declaratives**. Roughly, a sentence says:</speaker>
**<font size="+2">Who/what does what / is how / to whom / where…</font>**

So you have **“what we’re talking about”** and **“what we’re saying about it.”**

<speaker sprite="sor1">Sure — “I eat this”: **I** and **this** are the “what,” **eat** is the “doing.”</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc5">Borrowing logic vocabulary, we’ll call those **arguments (sumti)** and the **predicate (selbri)**.</speaker>

### Exercise

- Split these English sentences into sumti and selbri.
  1. That bird is blue.
  1. The man is drinking at the bar.
  1. This person is a woman.

<speaker sprite="sor5">Let me think… **A sentence is made of selbri and sumti.** The selbri is the “does/is” part; sumti are the “what” parts. Right?</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc5">Exactly.</speaker>
<speaker sprite="sor2">Okay, next.</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc1">Every Lojban predicate has **place slots** labelled **x1, x2, …**:</speaker>

- x1 is / does …  
- x1 does x2 …  
- x1 is x2-ish to x3 …  
- etc.

Think of **x1, x2, …** as numbered holes for sumti.

**Put sumti in numerical order with the selbri in the middle — that’s a sentence.** Easy, right?

<speaker sprite="sor1">Like:</speaker>
**[x1] [selbri] [x2] [x3] …**

<speaker sprite="sor5">Where does the selbri go *exactly*?</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc1">**Anywhere** — but most people put it **between x1 and x2** (like **SVO**).</speaker>
<speaker sprite="sor2">Kind of English-like.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc4">Right — a familiar default. Here are sumti you can use already:</speaker>
<dl class="valsi">
<dt>mi</dt>
<dd>I / me</dd>
<dt>do</dt>
<dd>you</dd>
<dt>zo'e</dt>
<dd>something unspecified / “whatever”</dd>
<dt>ti</dt>
<dd>this</dd>
<dt>ta</dt>
<dd>that (near you)</dd>
<dt>tu</dt>
<dd>that (yonder)</dd>
<dt>ra</dt>
<dd>he / she / it / they (in context)</dd>
</dl>

**Some predicates:**

<dl class="valsi">
<dt>ninmu</dt>
<dd>x1 is a woman</dd>
<dt>citka</dt>
<dd>x1 eats x2</dd>
<dt>zunle</dt>
<dd>x1 is to the left of x2 from viewpoint x3</dd>
<dt>sanli</dt>
<dd>x1 stands on x2 using legs/support x3</dd>
<dt>nelci</dt>
<dd>x1 likes x2</dd>
</dl>

### Exercise

- Translate into Lojban:
  1. I am a woman.
  1. You eat this.
  1. She is to the left of that (from my perspective).
  1. He stands there (with some legs/support).
  1. I like you.

<speaker sprite="sor1">Line up sumti, tuck in the selbri…</speaker>

1. **mi ninmu**  
2. **do citka ti**  
3. **ra zunle tu mi**  
4. **ra sanli tu zo'e**  
5. **mi nelci do**

<speaker sprite="koc5">Perfect. We took time on vocabulary on purpose — but forming the sentence is just ordering.</speaker>
<speaker sprite="sor2">Honestly maybe simpler than English for things like “she’s to my left of that.”</speaker>
