## Lesson 4. Basic sentence structure

<speaker sprite="sor2">First the sounds, then the word types... Alright, Koshon, lay that Lojban grammar on me! I'm ready for the "Super Grammar"!</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc1">Let's start with basic **statements**. Think of a sentence as saying:

**<font size="+2">Who or what / does what / is like what / to whom / where...</font>**

Basically, you have **"the thing we're talking about"** and **"what we're saying about it."**</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor1">Makes sense. Like "I eat this"—**I** and **this** are the "things," and **eat** is the "doing."</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc5">Exactly. Borrowing terms from logic, we call those "things" **arguments (sumti)** and the "doing/being" part the **predicate (selbri)**.</speaker>

### Exercise

- Identify the **sumti** and **selbri** in these English sentences:
  1. That bird is blue.
  1. The man is drinking at the bar.
  1. This person is a woman.

<speaker sprite="sor5">Let me see... **A sentence is built from a selbri and its sumti.** The selbri is the "is/does" part, and the sumti are the "who/what" parts. Right?</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc5">Spot on.</speaker>
<speaker sprite="sor2">Okay, next.</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc1">Every Lojban predicate has specific **place slots** labeled **x1, x2, x3, ...**:

- **x1** is / does ...
- **x1** does **x2** ...
- **x1** is **x2**-ish to **x3** ...
- and so on.

Think of **x1, x2, etc.** as numbered "holes" that you plug sumti into.

**To make a sentence, just put the sumti in their numbered order and tuck the selbri in the middle.** Simple, right?</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor1">Like:

**[x1] [selbri] [x2] [x3] …**</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor5">Wait, where *exactly* does the selbri go?</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc1">Strictly speaking, it can go **almost anywhere**—but most people put it **between x1 and x2**, much like English **SVO** (Subject-Verb-Object).</speaker>
<speaker sprite="sor2">That feels pretty natural.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc4">Right — a familiar default. Here are sumti you can use already:

<dl class="valsi">
<dt>mi</dt>
<dd>I / me</dd>
<dt>do</dt>
<dd>you</dd>
<dt>zo'e</dt>
<dd>something unspecified / “whatever”</dd>
<dt>ti</dt>
<dd>this</dd>
<dt>ta</dt>
<dd>that (near you)</dd>
<dt>tu</dt>
<dd>that (yonder)</dd>
<dt>ra</dt>
<dd>he / she / it / they (in context)</dd>
</dl>

**Some predicates:**

<dl class="valsi">
<dt>ninmu</dt>
<dd>x1 is a woman</dd>
<dt>citka</dt>
<dd>x1 eats x2</dd>
<dt>zunle</dt>
<dd>x1 is to the left of x2 from viewpoint x3</dd>
<dt>sanli</dt>
<dd>x1 stands on x2 using legs/support x3</dd>
<dt>nelci</dt>
<dd>x1 likes x2</dd>
</dl></speaker>

### Exercise

- Translate into Lojban:
  1. I am a woman.
  1. You eat this.
  1. She is to the left of that (from my perspective).
  1. He stands there (with some legs/support).
  1. I like you.

<speaker sprite="sor1">So, line up the sumti, tuck in the selbri...

1. **mi ninmu**
2. **do citka ti**
3. **ra zunle tu mi**
4. **ra sanli tu zo'e**
5. **mi nelci do**

<speaker sprite="koc5">Perfect! We're taking it slow with the vocabulary, but you've already mastered the basic sentence structure.</speaker>
<speaker sprite="sor2">Honestly, this might even be simpler than English for things like "they're to my left of that."</speaker>
