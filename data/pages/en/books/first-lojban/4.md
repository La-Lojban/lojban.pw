## Lesson 4. Basic sentence structure

<speaker sprite="sor3">First the sounds, then the word types... Alright, Koshon, lay that Lojban grammar on me! I'm ready for the "Super Grammar"!</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc1">Let's start with basic _statements_. Think of a sentence as saying:

<font size="+2">Who or what / does what / is like what / to whom / where...</font>

Basically, you have _"the thing we're talking about"_ and _"what we're saying about it."_</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor1">Makes sense. Like "I eat this"—_I_ and _this_ are the "things," and _eat_ is the "doing."</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc5">Exactly. Borrowing terms from logic, we call those "things" _arguments_ (_sumti_) and the "doing/being" part the _predicate_ (_selbri_).</speaker>

### Exercise

- Identify the _sumti_ and _selbri_ in these English sentences:
  1. That bird is blue.
  1. The man is drinking at the bar.
  1. This person is a woman.

<speaker sprite="sor5">Let me see... A sentence is built from a _selbri_ and its _sumti_. The _selbri_ is the "is/does" part, and the _sumti_ are the "who/what" parts. Right?</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc5">Spot on.</speaker>
<speaker sprite="sor1">Okay, next.</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc2">Every Lojban predicate has specific _place slots_ labeled **x<sub>1</sub>, x<sub>2</sub>, x<sub>3</sub>, ...**:

- **x<sub>1</sub>** is / does ...
- **x<sub>1</sub>** does **x<sub>2</sub>** ...
- **x<sub>1</sub>** is **x<sub>2</sub>**-ish to **x<sub>3</sub>** ...
- and so on.

Think of **x<sub>1</sub>, x<sub>2</sub>, etc.** as numbered "holes" that you plug sumti into.

To make a sentence, just put the _sumti_ in their numbered order and tuck the _selbri_ in the middle. Simple, right?</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor1">Like:

**[x<sub>1</sub>] [selbri] [x<sub>2</sub>] [x<sub>3</sub>] …**</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor2">Wait, where *exactly* does the selbri go?</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc2">Strictly speaking, the _selbri_ can go _almost anywhere_—but most people put it _between_ **x<sub>1</sub>** _and_ **x<sub>2</sub>**, much like English _SVO_ (_Subject-Verb-Object_).</speaker>
<speaker sprite="sor7">That feels pretty natural.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc2">Right — a familiar default. Here are sumti you can use already:

<dl class="valsi">
<dt>mi</dt>
<dd>I / me</dd>
<dt>do</dt>
<dd>you</dd>
<dt>zo'e</dt>
<dd>something unspecified / “whatever”</dd>
<dt>ti</dt>
<dd>this</dd>
<dt>ta</dt>
<dd>that (near you)</dd>
<dt>tu</dt>
<dd>that (yonder)</dd>
<dt>ra</dt>
<dd>he / she / it / they (in context)</dd>
</dl>

Some predicates:

<dl class="valsi">
<dt>ninmu</dt>
<dd>x<sub>1</sub> is a woman</dd>
<dt>citka</dt>
<dd>x<sub>1</sub> eats x<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dt>zunle</dt>
<dd>x<sub>1</sub> is to the left of x<sub>2</sub> from viewpoint x<sub>3</sub></dd>
<dt>sanli</dt>
<dd>x<sub>1</sub> stands on x<sub>2</sub> using legs/support x<sub>3</sub></dd>
<dt>nelci</dt>
<dd>x<sub>1</sub> likes x<sub>2</sub></dd>
</dl></speaker>

### Exercise

- Translate into Lojban:
  1. I am a woman.
  1. You eat this.
  1. She is to the left of that (from my perspective).
  1. He stands there (with some legs/support).
  1. I like you.

<speaker sprite="sor1">So, line up the sumti, tuck in the selbri...

1. **mi ninmu**
2. **do citka ti**
3. **ra zunle tu mi**
4. **ra sanli tu zo'e**
5. **mi nelci do**

</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc5">Perfect! We're taking it slow with the vocabulary, but you've already mastered the basic sentence structure.</speaker>
<speaker sprite="sor7">Honestly, this might even be simpler than English for things like "they're to my left of that."</speaker>
