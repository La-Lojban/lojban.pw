## Lesson 34. Tanru brackets: **bo**, **ke … ke'e**, and **co**

<speaker sprite="koc2">Long time no see! Sevan asked me to guest-lecture on tanru grouping.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sev4">Koshon knows this better than I do. Also, Sora listens to Koshon.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor3">I do _not_… okay maybe a little.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc5">Recall from Lesson 6: tanru group left to right by default.

> **blabi mlatu zdani** = ((blabi mlatu) zdani)

That means "a white-cat house" — the house is cat-like (white-cat-type house). _Not_ "a white (cat-house)" (a house for white cats).</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor1">Right, and we got burned by that once. So how do we change the grouping?</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc2">Three tools: **bo**, **ke/ke'e**, and **co**.</speaker>

### **bo** — raise binding priority

<speaker sprite="koc1">**bo** placed _between_ two selbri binds them tighter than the default. Think of it as the multiplication sign in arithmetic: <span style="white-space:nowrap">`2 + 3 × 4`</span> evaluates differently from <span style="white-space:nowrap">`(2 + 3) × 4`</span>, and **bo** is the × sign.</speaker>

bo
: Makes the two selbri on either side bind before anything else.

> **blabi mlatu bo zdani**
> = blabi (mlatu **bo** zdani)
> = blabi (mlatu-zdani)
> _A white cat-house._ — the house is white; it's a cat-house kind of thing.

> **blabi bo mlatu zdani**
> = (blabi **bo** mlatu) zdani
> = (blabi-mlatu) zdani
> _A white-cat house._ — same as the default, emphasized.

<speaker sprite="sor2">Oh! So `blabi mlatu bo zdani` finally puts the house in charge of being white, not the cat.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc4">Exactly. **bo** is the quick way to say "glue these two together first."</speaker>

### **ke … ke'e** — explicit parentheses

<speaker sprite="koc2">If **bo** is the × sign, **ke … ke'e** are actual parentheses. Everything between them is treated as one unit before any surrounding selbri are considered.</speaker>

ke
: Open grouping bracket for tanru.

ke'e
: Close grouping bracket (terminator for **ke**).

> **blabi ke mlatu zdani ke'e**
> = blabi ( mlatu zdani )
> _A white cat-house._ (Same meaning as the **bo** example above.)

<speaker sprite="sor5">When would I use **ke** instead of **bo**?</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc5">When grouping more than two pieces. Watch:</speaker>

> **xekri ke melbi kerfa ke'e ke cinla birka ke'e bo ninmu**
> = xekri (melbi kerfa) (cinla birka) **bo** ninmu
> _A woman with black beautiful-hair and slender arms._
> Structure: ((xekri (melbi kerfa)) ((cinla birka) ninmu))

kerfa
: x<sub>1</sub> is the hair/fur of body x<sub>2</sub> at location x<sub>3</sub>

cinla
: x<sub>1</sub> is thin/slender in dimension x<sub>2</sub>, by standard x<sub>3</sub>

birka
: x<sub>1</sub> is an arm of body x<sub>2</sub>

<speaker sprite="sor2">So **ke'e** is the right terminator for **ke**. Not **ku** or anything else.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc3">Right. And **ke'e** is often _optional_ in practice — if it's at the very end of the tanru before a sumti, the parser can figure it out. But when you're nesting multiple brackets, explicit **ke'e** is much safer.</speaker>

### **co** — flip the modification direction

<speaker sprite="koc1">**co** is the trickiest of the three. It does two things together:</speaker>

1. <u>Makes everything to its left the _modifier_</u> (lower priority).
2. <u>Makes everything to its right the _head_</u> (the modified selbri).

In other words: **co** reverses the usual left-modifies-right pattern.

co
: Inverts tanru modification direction; what follows **co** is the head.

> **zdani co blabi** → _a white house_ (zdani is now the thing being described; blabi is the modifier)
> **bunre mlatu co nimre jisra nelci** → _an orange-juice-liking brown cat_

nimre
: x<sub>1</sub> is a citrus/mandarin of species x<sub>2</sub>

jisra
: x<sub>1</sub> is juice/liquid from x<sub>2</sub>

<speaker sprite="sor1">Why would you ever want to reverse the direction? That sounds confusing.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc4">It becomes useful when you want to attach a long modifier _phrase_ (one that includes **be/bei** sumti slots) to a simpler head. Without **co**, that gets unwieldy:</speaker>

> Without **co** (verbose):
> **tu klama be la .latcmatcad. bei le cmana ke ladru nelci ke blabi mlatu**

> With **co** (clean):
> **tu ladru nelci ke blabi mlatu co klama be la .latcmatcad. bei le cmana**
> _That thing is a milk-loving white-cat that goes from the mountain to Latcmatcad._

<speaker sprite="sor7">Oh — **co** lets you keep the main concept (klama — "goes") on the right where it's easy to read, and pile the modifiers to the left of **co**. The arguments after **co** then belong to **klama**, not to the stuff on the left.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc5">That's the third feature of **co**: the sumti following **co**'s right-side selbri fill _that_ selbri's places, not the overall tanru's. It's a convenience for readable tanru with embedded sumti.</speaker>

### Tanru poetry challenge

<speaker sprite="koc1">Now for something fun. Write a short poem using _only selbri_ — no sumti, no tags. Just predicates strung together with **.i** between them. You can use **bo**, **ke**, and **co** to shape your tanru. The brevity forces you to let the predicates carry everything.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sev4">Koshon calls this _tanru pemci_ — predicate poetry.</speaker>

pemci
: x<sub>1</sub> is a poem with property x<sub>2</sub>, by author x<sub>3</sub>, for audience x<sub>4</sub>

<speaker sprite="koc2">Here's an example:</speaker>

> **.i xendo solri bo gusni canci**  
> **.i nicte manku klama**  
> **.i cladu cliva**  
> **.i smaji**

> _(loose translation: The gentle sunlight fades. Night-darkness comes. Loudness departs. Silence.)_

xendo
: x<sub>1</sub> is kind/gentle to x<sub>2</sub>

solri
: x<sub>1</sub> is the sun/star of planet x<sub>2</sub>

gusni
: x<sub>1</sub> is light illuminating x<sub>2</sub> from source x<sub>3</sub>

canci
: x<sub>1</sub> disappears from x<sub>2</sub> (place/view)

cladu
: x<sub>1</sub> is loud/noisy to observer x<sub>2</sub>, by standard x<sub>3</sub>

smaji
: x<sub>1</sub> is quiet/silent to observer x<sub>2</sub>, by standard x<sub>3</sub>

<speaker sprite="sor2">No nouns, no pronouns, no time words — just pure predicates. And it _works_. Because Lojban predicates already contain the "is" and "does" without needing extra words.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc4">Tense is optional. Number is optional. Subject is optional. Tanru is ambiguous by design. All of that ambiguity becomes _atmosphere_ in poetry.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor5">Okay I'll try one:

> **.i barda mlatu**  
> **.i cmalu mlatu**  
> **.i mutce melbi**  
> **.i gleki munje**

> _(Big cat. Small cat. Very beautiful. Happy world.)_</speaker>
