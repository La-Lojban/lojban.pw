## Lesson 10. Abstractions (noun clauses)

<speaker sprite="koc1">We're almost halfway through! This is a great time to learn about **abstractions**—what we call "noun clauses" or "that..." clauses in English.</speaker>
From [Lesson 5](/en/books/first-lojban/5), recall:

> **la .kocon. djuno lo du'u la .soran. zvati la .latcmatcad. kei ku**

  — Koshon knows that Sora is in Latcmatcad.

<dl class="valsi">
<dt>djuno</dt>
<dd>x1 knows proposition x2 …</dd>
</dl>

> **mi nelci lo nu cadzu kei ku**
> _I like walking._


> **mi gleki lo nu mi penmi do la .latcmatcad. kei ku**
> _I'm happy that I met you in Latcmatcad._


<speaker sprite="sor1">I see the pattern:</speaker>

- **lo nu [sentence] kei ku** — the **event** where [sentence] happens.
- **lo du'u [sentence] kei ku** — the **proposition** that [sentence] is true.

<speaker sprite="koc1">Exactly. While English often just uses "that" for both, Lojban distinguishes between an **event** and a **proposition** (along with several others).</speaker>
<speaker sprite="sor5">Wait, what exactly is a "proposition"?</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc4">Think of it as the "content" of an idea—the kind of thing you believe, doubt, or claim as true.</speaker>
<speaker sprite="sor1">A bit fuzzy, but I think I get the gist.</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc1">Lojban dictionaries will tell you whether a word normally takes **nu** or **du'u** in its slots. You'll develop a feel for it as we go.

The general pattern is:
**lo [Abstractor] [sentence] kei ku**

The word in the middle is called an **abstractor** (or a **NU-series cmavo**). We'll start with just **nu** and **du'u**.</speaker>

<dl class="drani">
<dt>lo nu</dt>
<dd>article + abstractor: event of the following bridi</dd>
<dt>lo du'u</dt>
<dd>article + abstractor: proposition of the following bridi</dd>
<dt>kei</dt>
<dd>terminator closing the abstraction’s bridi</dd>
</dl>

<speaker sprite="koc1">There are several others like **ka**, **ni**, **si'o**, and so on, but let's stick to these two for now.</speaker>
<speaker sprite="sor6">It still feels a little... well, abstract.</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc2">Practice:</speaker>

### Exercise

- Translate to English:
  1. **mi badri lo nu le ninmu ku morsi kei ku**  
  2. **lo nu citka lo plise ku kei ku pluka le cipni ku**  
  3. **le nanmu ku birti lo du'u le plise ku kukte kei ku**  
  4. **lo nu tadni la .lojban. kei ku cinri mi**  
  5. **mi jinvi lo du'u do melbi kei ku**

<dl class="valsi">
<dt>badri</dt><dd>x1 is sad about x2</dd>
<dt>morsi</dt><dd>x1 is dead</dd>
<dt>plise</dt><dd>x1 is an apple …</dd>
<dt>pluka</dt><dd>x1 pleases x2 …</dd>
<dt>cipni</dt><dd>x1 is a bird …</dd>
<dt>birti</dt><dd>x1 is certain proposition x2 holds</dd>
<dt>kukte</dt><dd>x1 is tasty to x2</dd>
<dt>tadni</dt><dd>x1 studies x2</dd>
<dt>cinri</dt><dd>x1 interests x2</dd>
<dt>jinvi</dt><dd>x1 opines that x2 …</dd>
<dt>melbi</dt><dd>x1 is beautiful …</dd>
</dl>

<speaker sprite="sor2">Roughly:</speaker>

1. I'm sad that the woman died.
2. Eating that apple pleases the bird.
3. The man is certain (of the proposition) that the apple is tasty.
4. Studying Lojban is interesting to me.
5. I think you are beautiful.

<speaker sprite="koc5">Excellent! You can think of them as building **noun clauses** with a clear closing tag (**kei**).</speaker>
<speaker sprite="sor1">**kei ku** feels like a lot of typing... So **nu [sentence] kei** is basically a selbri by itself?</speaker>
<speaker sprite="koc5">Technically, yes—abstractors take a full sentence and build a new predicate out of it. But in practice, you'll almost always see them wrapped in **lo ... ku** to turn them into arguments (sumti).</speaker>
<speaker sprite="sor2">So **nu** means "...is an event" and **du'u** means "...is a proposition." If you say I don't need to overthink it, I won't!</speaker>
