## Lesson 17. Questions

<speaker sprite="koc5">Today's topic: _questions_! There are two main families: _truth questions_ (_yes_/_no_) and _fill-in-the-blank questions_ (for things or actions). First, a quick test, Sora—translate: _"I am not a human."_</speaker>

remna

### Truth questions: **xu**

<speaker sprite="koc5">Good—glad to see you haven't forgotten **na**. To ask a _yes_/_no_ _question_, just place the word **xu** at the beginning of the sentence (or right after the **.i** marker).</speaker>

xu
: An attitudinal word that asks whether the sentence is true.

> **[.i] xu lo vi prenu cu mamta mi**
> _Is this person my mother?_

mamta
: x<sub>1</sub> is x<sub>2</sub>’s mother

<speaker sprite="sor4">What kind of example is that...? And what's an _"attitudinal"_?</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc2">_Attitudinals_ express emotions or attitudes—we’ll cover them in detail in the [finale](/en/books/first-lojban/#lesson-25-attitudinals-ui). For now, the important question is: how do you _answer_? You can use **go'i** to mean "Yes, that's right" or **na go'i** for "No, that's not it."</speaker>

> **.i xu lo vi prenu cu mamta mi**
> _Is this my mother?_

> **― .i na go'i .i tu mamta do**
> _No, I'm not. **That** woman over there is your mother._

go'i
: pro-bridi; repeats the previous bridi.

<speaker sprite="sor2">(Wait, so she *wasn't* your mother?) So **go'i** basically _repeats_ the previous statement, right?</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc5">Exactly. Grammatically, **go'i** acts as a _selbri_ that means "the same as the last sentence." So **na go'i** just means "that sentence isn't true." This is usually all you need for answering _yes_/_no_ questions.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor5">Got it. English _yes_/_no_ flips on _negative questions_ — how does Lojban handle that?</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc2">It's a common stumbling block! In Lojban, **go'i** echoes the _exact_ previous statement. If you're asked a negative question like _"Is it true you're NOT my mother?"_, a plain "yes" or "no" can be confusing. To be perfectly clear, you can use **ja'a go'i** to strongly affirm the positive version of the sentence.</speaker>

> **.i xu lo vi prenu na mamta mi**
> _Is this person not my mother?_

> **ja'a go'i**
> _(affirms the positive inner bridi — roughly “yes, they are your mother.”)_

### Wh- questions: **ma**

<speaker sprite="koc2">For _"fill-in-the-blank" questions_, use **ma**. You just place **ma** exactly where the thing you're asking about would go. You can even use several in one sentence! The listener answers by giving the missing information in the same order.</speaker>

ma
: Asks a question that needs a thing (sumti) as an answer.

> **.i mi ca catlu ma**
> _What am I looking at?_

> **― .i lo patfu**
> _Father._

catlu
: x<sub>1</sub> looks at / watches x<sub>2</sub>

<speaker sprite="koc2">To ask _when, where, why, or how_, just combine a tag with **ma**. For example: **ca ma** (_when_), **bu'u ma** (_where_), **ki'u ma** (_why_), or **ta'i ma** (_how_—from **tadji**, meaning _method_).</speaker>

ca ma
: when

bu'u ma
: where (at …)

ki'u ma
: why (with what reason)

ta'i ma
: how (by what method)

### **mo** — selbri question

<speaker sprite="koc2">**mo** replaces the _selbri_: “What is x<sub>1</sub> / what is going on?” Answer with a _selbri_ — same idea as **ma**, different slot.</speaker>

mo
: Asks a question that needs an action or attribute (selbri) as an answer.

> **.i lo vi prenu cu mo**
> _Who or what is this person? (What's their relationship to the topic?)_

> **― .i ja'a patfu**
> _They are (truly) my father._

<speaker sprite="koc5">Exactly. **mo** can even be used inside _tanru_—for instance, **mo nanmu** means "What kind of man?"</speaker>
