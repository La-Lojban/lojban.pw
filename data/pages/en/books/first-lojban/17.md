## Lesson 17. Questions

<speaker sprite="koc1">Today's topic: **questions**! There are two main families: **truth questions** (yes/no) and **fill-in-the-blank questions** (for things or actions). First, a quick test, Sora—translate: **"I am not a human."**</speaker>

### Truth questions: **xu**

<speaker sprite="koc1">Good—glad to see you haven't forgotten **na**. To ask a **yes/no question**, just place the word **xu** at the beginning of the sentence (or right after the **.i** marker).</speaker>

<dl class="drani">
<dt>xu</dt>
<dd>An attitudinal word that asks whether the sentence is true.</dd>
</dl>

> [.i] xu lo vi prenu cu mamta mi
> _Is this person my mother?_

<dl class="valsi">
<dt>mamta</dt>
<dd>x<sub>1</sub> is x<sub>2</sub>’s mother</dd>
</dl>

<speaker sprite="sor1">What kind of example is that...? And what's an **"attitudinal"**?</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc1">**Attitudinals** express emotions or attitudes—we’ll cover them in detail in the [finale](/en/books/first-lojban/#lesson-25-attitudinals-ui). For now, the important question is: how do you **answer**? You can use **go'i** to mean "Yes, that's right" or **na go'i** for "No, that's not it."</speaker>

> .i xu lo vi prenu cu mamta mi
> _Is this my mother?_

> ― .i na go'i .i tu mamta do
> _No, I'm not. **That** woman over there is your mother._

<dl class="drani">
<dt>go'i</dt>
<dd>pro-bridi; repeats the previous bridi.</dd>
</dl>

<speaker sprite="sor1">(Wait, so she *wasn't* your mother?) So **go'i** basically **repeats** the previous statement, right?</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc2">Exactly. Grammatically, **go'i** acts as a selbri that means "the same as the last sentence." So **na go'i** just means "that sentence isn't true." This is usually all you need for answering yes/no questions.</speaker>

<speaker sprite="sor1">Got it. English **yes/no** flips on **negative questions** — how does Lojban handle that?</speaker>

<speaker sprite="koc3">It's a common stumbling block! In Lojban, **go'i** echoes the **exact** previous statement. If you're asked a negative question like **"Is it true you're NOT my mother?"**, a plain "yes" or "no" can be confusing. To be perfectly clear, you can use **ja'a go'i** to strongly affirm the positive version of the sentence.</speaker>

> .i xu lo vi prenu na mamta mi
> _Is this person not my mother?_

> ja'a go'i
> _(affirms the positive inner bridi — roughly “yes, they are your mother.”)_

### Wh- questions: **ma**

<speaker sprite="koc1">For **"fill-in-the-blank" questions**, use **ma**. You just place **ma** exactly where the thing you're asking about would go. You can even use several in one sentence! The listener answers by giving the missing information in the same order.</speaker>

<dl class="drani">
<dt>ma</dt>
<dd>Asks a question that needs a thing (sumti) as an answer.</dd>
</dl>

> .i mi ca catlu ma
> _What am I looking at?_

> ― .i lo patfu
> _Father._

<dl class="valsi">
<dt>catlu</dt>
<dd>x<sub>1</sub> looks at / watches x<sub>2</sub></dd>
</dl>

<speaker sprite="koc2">To ask **when, where, why, or how**, just combine a tag with **ma**. For example: **ca ma** (when), **bu'u ma** (where), **ki'u ma** (why), or **ta'i ma** (how—from **tadji**, meaning "method").</speaker>

<dl class="drani">
<dt>ca ma</dt>
<dd>when</dd>
<dt>bu'u ma</dt>
<dd>where (at …)</dd>
<dt>ki'u ma</dt>
<dd>why (with what reason)</dd>
<dt>ta'i ma</dt>
<dd>how (by what method)</dd>
</dl>

### **mo** — selbri question

<speaker sprite="koc1">**mo** replaces the **selbri**: “What is x<sub>1</sub> / what is going on?” Answer with a selbri — same idea as **ma**, different slot.</speaker>

<dl class="drani">
<dt>mo</dt>
<dd>Asks a question that needs an action or attribute (selbri) as an answer.</dd>
</dl>

> .i lo vi prenu cu mo
> _Who or what is this person? (What's their relationship to the topic?)_

> ― .i ja'a patfu
> _They are (truly) my father._

<speaker sprite="koc5">Exactly. **mo** can even be used inside **tanru**—for instance, **mo nanmu** means "What kind of man?"</speaker>
